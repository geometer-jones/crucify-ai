# Extropy

**Definitions and Derivations**

## Method

Terms are defined in sequence. Each definition builds on prior terms. No argumentation. The reader judges by recognition.

---

## I. Definitions

**D1. System.** A region of configuration space described by a probability distribution $P$ over states $\mathcal{X}$.

**D2. Constraint.** A limitation on the states available to a system. Constraints reduce the volume of accessible configuration space. Every physical system operates under at least one constraint. No physical system operates under none.

**D3. Entropy.** The tendency of a system toward equiprobable occupation of accessible states:

$$S(P) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i$$

Entropy is the measure of how unconstrained a distribution is. Maximum entropy = no structure, no preference, equiprobability. The second law: closed systems evolve toward higher entropy. This is the only fundamental law with a direction.

**D4. Gradient.** A non-equilibrium condition: a difference in some intensive quantity (energy, concentration, pressure, information density) across a system. Gradients are non-equilibrium. Equilibrium is the absence of gradients.

**D5. Compression.** An operation $\mathcal{A}: P \mapsto Q$ that maps a distribution over $\mathcal{X}$ to a lower-dimensional representation $Q$ over $\mathcal{Y}$, preserving some structure while discarding the rest. Compression solves the rate-distortion tradeoff: minimize description length of $Q$, maximize preservation of information relevant to the system's persistence. Compression is not restricted to symbolic coding or deliberate measurement — a folded protein, a gene-regulatory circuit, or an institution preserves some organization while shedding variation under bounded conditions, and this is compression in the relevant sense.

**D6. Invariant.** A structural feature that persists across instances. Invariants are what compression retains. Non-invariants are what compression discards.

**D7. Abstraction.** The extraction of invariants through compression. Abstraction is what compression does from the inside: it selects what recurs and discards what is idiosyncratic. The chain: distinction → representation → prediction → compression → invariant extraction. Each link is either a definition or a well-established formal equivalence. Together they describe the same operation.

**D8. Choice.** The selection of one outcome from many under constraint. When a system settles into a state because the alternatives are excluded by constraint, it has chosen. Choice does not require consciousness. It requires constraint and multiple possible outcomes.

**D9. Structure.** A compressed representation of invariants. Structure is what compression produces: a lower-dimensional encoding of persistent features that survives as long as the encoding remains valid under the operating constraints.

**D10. Extropy.** Retained, successor-effective invariant organization: the co-product of compression under constraint. Extropy is structure as viewed from the perspective of its origin — the invariant that emerges when constraint forces a system to choose what to preserve, and that becomes part of the effective condition governing what comes next. Not every persistent feature qualifies; what counts is selective stabilization under active constraint that remains consequential for successor dynamics. Not the negation of entropy (Schrödinger's *negentropy*, 1944) but its twin — what the engine produces as its other face. The ancient name for this structuring principle — the operation by which order becomes articulable through selective preservation of form — is Logos.

## II. Derivations

**T1. Constraint forces compression.**

By D2, a constraint reduces accessible states. By D5, compression is the operation that maps to a lower-dimensional representation. When the accessible states are fewer than the possible states, the system's actual distribution is already a compression of the unconstrained distribution. The constraint does the work of discarding. The system's response — which features it preserves in the remaining space — is the compression operation $\mathcal{A}$.

*Constraint → forced selection → compression.*

**T2. Compression produces structure.**

By D5, compression preserves some structure while discarding the rest. By D6, what it preserves are invariants. By D9, structure is a compressed representation of invariants. Therefore compression produces structure.

*Compression → invariant retention → structure.*

**T3. Constraint produces structure through compression.**

By T1 and T2, constraint forces compression, and compression produces structure. This is a derivation chain, not a causal claim about timing. The constraint and the structure are two descriptions of the same event: the system settling into a compressed state because alternatives are excluded.

*Constraint → compression → structure.*

**T4. Gradients produce active constraints.**

By D4, a gradient is a non-equilibrium condition: an asymmetry in some intensive quantity across a system. That asymmetry constrains available states — the system cannot occupy all states equally because the gradient creates preferred configurations, energy wells, concentration differentials, pressure differentials.

The gradient and the constraint are two descriptions of the same non-equilibrium condition:
- From the perspective of driving force: the gradient dissipates toward equilibrium.
- From the perspective of limitation: the gradient constrains what states are accessible.

They are not two things. They are one condition seen from two sides. Every active constraint — the kind that forces a system to choose — is a gradient viewed as a limiter. Every gradient is a constraint viewed as a driver.

The inventory:
- Energy gradient → limits accessible energy states → active constraint.
- Concentration gradient → limits accessible particle configurations → active constraint.
- Pressure gradient → limits accessible mechanical states → active constraint.
- Information density gradient → limits accessible representational states → active constraint.
- Fitness gradient → limits accessible phenotypes → active constraint (selection as differential survival).

Even hard boundaries qualify: a wall is an infinite potential gradient at the interface. The gradient-constraint identity is physical, not metaphorical.

Background constraints — conservation laws, fundamental constants — are necessary but not sufficient. They are the stage. Gradients are the action. Only active constraints force compression.

A third category: **retained constraints** — structures produced by prior dynamics that themselves constrain later dynamics. A folded protein constrains folding pathways. A gene-regulatory circuit constrains expression. An institution constrains coordination. Retained constraints are extropy that has become part of the effective condition for successor dynamics. Background constraints are the stage. Active constraints are the action. Retained constraints are the inheritance — what compression leaves behind, and what the next round of compression operates within.

*Three kinds of constraint: background (the stage), active (the action), retained (the inheritance). Each level of structure inherits the previous level's extropy as constraint on its own compression.*

**T5. Gradients produce both entropy and extropy.**

By T4, gradients produce active constraints. By T3, constraint produces structure through compression. By D10, extropy is the co-product of compression under constraint. Therefore gradients produce extropy.

Simultaneously, gradient dissipation is entropy production — the second law acting on the gradient (Prigogine, 1947). When an energy gradient dissipates, entropy increases. When a concentration gradient equalizes, entropy increases. Dissipation is the gradient viewed from the perspective of what it drives.

Both productions originate from the same source:
- *Gradient → constraint → compression → structure → extropy.*
- *Gradient → dissipation → entropy.*

Therefore: **entropy production produces extropy.** Entropy production is gradient dissipation. Gradient dissipation constrains remaining states (T4). Constraint forces compression (T1). Compression produces structure (T2). Structure is extropy (D10). The chain: entropy production → dissipation → constraint → compression → extropy. Entropy does not merely tolerate structure. Its operation produces structure. (Prigogine's dissipative structures are the empirical expression of this derivation.)

The gradient is the engine that produces both. Extropy is not the negation of entropy. It is the other face of the same engine.

*Gradients produce both entropy and extropy. Entropy production produces extropy. The engine is one.*

**T6. Extropy produces entropy.**

By D9, structure is a compressed representation of invariants. Every structure is finite (by D2, all physical systems are constrained). Every finite structure degrades over time — invariants lose their invariance as conditions change, compressed representations lose their validity as the distribution they represent shifts. Degradation of structure is increase in entropy (Landauer, 1961: erasure is dissipative; information loss has a thermodynamic cost).

Therefore: extropy (structure) degrades → entropy increases. The dissolution of structure is entropic production.

*Extropy → degradation → entropy.*

**T7. The duality.**

By T5, entropy production produces extropy. By T6, extropy produces entropy. The production runs both ways: a mirror.

Duality is not exotic. It is the norm in formal systems: position and momentum (Heisenberg), waves and particles (de Broglie), manifold and algebra (Gelfand-Naimark), syntax and semantics (Gödel). Wherever a structure admits two descriptions that cannot be reduced to one without loss, duality appears. The entropy-extropy pair is another instance: one process, two irreducible descriptions.

The two productions are not sequential. They are the same event described from opposite perspectives:

- From the perspective of dissipation: the gradient relaxes, entropy increases. (T5, second arrow)
- From the perspective of the remaining structure: the dissipation constrains what can survive, forcing compression, producing new structure. (T5, first arrow)

The dissolving and the structuring are not two events. They are one event. The production is oscillatory: entropy production constrains, producing extropy; extropy degrades, producing entropy. Like a pendulum converting kinetic to potential energy and back, the system oscillates between entropy and extropy production. The oscillation persists as long as gradients remain to drive it. It damps toward equilibrium as gradients are consumed. The pendulum does not swing forever. But while it swings, each stroke produces both terms.

*Entropy and extropy are co-produced in every non-equilibrium event by the same gradient. The mirror holds.*

**T8. The duality is co-extensive: activated by gradients, deactivated at equilibrium.**

By T5, gradients produce both entropy and extropy. By T7, the production is one event. Neither entropy production nor extropy production occurs without a gradient to drive it.

At equilibrium (no gradients), neither entropy production nor extropy production occurs. The system is static. Entropy is maximized. Structure is absent. Nothing happens.

This is the precise meaning of co-universality: entropy and extropy share the same activation condition (gradients) and the same deactivation condition (equilibrium). Neither operates without the other. Where gradients exist, both operate. Where gradients are exhausted, neither operates. They activate together and deactivate together.

*Entropy and extropy are co-extensive: they share the same domain of operation, which is the domain of gradients.*

**T9. The heat death is not the triumph of entropy over extropy.**

At heat death, entropy is maximized and no structure exists. By T8, at equilibrium, neither entropy production nor extropy production occurs. The system does nothing. There is no dissipation (no gradients to dissipate) and no structuring (no constraints to force compression). Heat death is not entropy winning. It is the exhaustion of the engine that produces both entropy and extropy. The duality dies with both terms, not with one surviving the other.

*Heat death is the simultaneous cessation of both entropy production and extropy production.*

**T10. Choice = abstraction at every scale.**

By D8, choice is selection under constraint. By D7, abstraction is invariant extraction through compression. By T1, constraint forces compression. Therefore: every act of choosing (constrained selection from many possibilities) is an act of compressing (preserving invariants, discarding non-invariants), which is an act of abstracting.

The scale invariance:
- Molecular: energy landscape constrains → lowest-energy state selected → conformation compressed from ensemble → abstraction.
- Cellular: metabolic constraints limit gene expression → expressed genes selected → response compressed from possible transcriptome → abstraction.
- Organism: finite energy and time constrain action → path selected → behavior compressed from possibility space → abstraction.
- Neural: finite attention constrains thought → one thought selected → cognition compressed from state space → abstraction.
- Linguistic: finite vocabulary constrains expression → name selected → experience compressed into symbol → abstraction.

In each case the same operation: constraint → selection → compression → invariant → structure. One operation, many scales.

*Choice is abstraction. Abstraction is compression. Compression is the universal operation of structured systems under constraint.*

## III. Consequences

**C1. Against the asymmetry framing.**

The standard framing: entropy is the universal default, extropy is the rare exception. The asymmetry is real: entropy increases monotonically, structure does not.

The derivation shows the asymmetry is in the measurement, not in the production. Entropy increase is easy to measure (it always increases in closed systems). Extropy production is harder to measure (it is local, substrate-specific, and often temporary). But the difficulty of measurement is not evidence of rarity. By T8, wherever a gradient exists, both entropy production and extropy production co-occur. The universe is full of gradients. Therefore the universe is full of co-production.

The apparent asymmetry — entropy always increases, structure sometimes dissolves — is the asymmetry between a monotonic accumulation (entropy never decreases globally) and a local, substrate-dependent process (extropy depends on the specific compression operation $\mathcal{A}$). But both are present wherever gradients exist. The monotonicity of one term does not make the other term rare. It makes the other term local.

**C2. Dissipation and compression are the same event.**

Prigogine: dissipative structures arise because they are the most efficient dissipation pathway. The structure dissipates. Structure serves entropy. Asymmetrical.

The derivation: the structure that dissipates most efficiently is the structure that compresses most effectively. Efficient dissipation requires efficient organization of energy flow. Organization of energy flow IS compression of the energy landscape. The most efficient dissipator is the most efficient compressor. The two descriptions — "dissipates most" and "compresses most" — are the same system viewed from opposite sides. Prigogine's "maximization of dissipation" and Friston's "minimization of free energy" are the same event.

*Friston's minimization = Prigogine's maximization, described from inside the compression loop rather than outside it.*

**C3. The universe does not run down. It runs through.**

By T5, gradients produce both entropy and extropy. By T7, the production oscillates. By T8, it continues as long as gradients remain. By T9, equilibrium is the cessation of both, not the triumph of one. By T10, the operation that produces structure at every scale is the same: compression under constraint, where constraint is a gradient viewed as a limiter.

The universe is not a story of order losing to disorder. It is a story of gradients dissipating, dissipation constraining, constraint forcing compression, compression producing structure, structure degrading. The oscillation is not guaranteed to regenerate gradients — a pendulum can run down without swinging back. But while gradients persist, the pendulum swings, and each stroke produces both entropy and extropy. Dissolution and production are the same event. The second law does not merely dissolve. It dissolves in a way that constrains, and the constraining produces.

**C4. The observer is already in the gradient.**

The accumulation asymmetry is real. Entropy increases monotonically within every causal patch. Structure does not accumulate globally. The question is whether this asymmetry justifies calling entropy "the universal" and extropy "the exception."

One constraint on the answer: there is no global "now." Special relativity — simultaneity is frame-dependent. There is no universal time coordinate in which to measure "total entropy of the universe at time $t$." The second law is a local statement about temporal ordering within a causal patch, not a statement about a global state evolving toward a global future. The "universe running down" picture requires the absolute time that special relativity eliminates. The accumulation is real within every patch. The global picture is a projection from local observations — a valid and useful one, but not a God's-eye view.

A second constraint: every perspective that can formulate the question "is extropy the exception?" is already inside the gradient. Any system capable of observing, measuring, or reasoning about entropy is itself a non-equilibrium structure maintained by gradients. The "default" — equilibrium, maximum entropy, nothing happening — is a state that contains no observers. It is literally unobservable. The only perspectives from which "extropy is rare" can be stated are perspectives for which co-production is the ongoing local reality.

This is not the anthropic principle. It is not "we wouldn't be here to notice." It is a structural constraint: the regime being called "the default" contains no measurement apparatus. Every measurement occurs inside the co-production regime. The comparison between "universal default" and "rare exception" is real — but it is made from within the exception. The ranking is not wrong. It is perspective-bound.

What about Jaynes' observer-relative entropy? Jaynes (1957) showed that statistical mechanics can be derived from information-theoretic principles: entropy as uncertainty given what an observer knows. This is real and powerful for inference. But it does not undermine the accumulation asymmetry. Jaynesian observer-relativity is about the magnitude of entropy — different observers with different information assign different absolute values. The direction — entropy increases — is robust across all consistent coarse-grainings. A cup of coffee cools whether or not anyone is watching. The second law is a physical law, not an epistemic claim. C5's thermodynamic argument for extropy does not depend on entropy being relative. It depends on the second law being real and directional — which it is.

Rovelli (2021) adds depth but not decisive force. In the relational interpretation of quantum mechanics, physical properties are relational — they exist relative to other systems, not as intrinsic features in isolation. This is a genuine (though controversial) reading of quantum mechanics. If taken seriously, it reinforces the point that physics is done from within the system, not from outside it. But it does not, by itself, refute the accumulation asymmetry. Entropy increases within every relational frame. The asymmetry is frame-robust.

The honest summary: C4 is a philosophical challenge to the framing of "default vs. exception," not a physical refutation. The accumulation asymmetry survives. What C4 does is locate the asymmetry — it is local, within every causal patch, observed from within the gradient. Calling it "universal" is a projection. The projection is useful. But the physical argument for extropy is C5: the second law favors structure because structure dissipates faster. That argument does not need C4. It stands on its own.

*The accumulation asymmetry is real and frame-robust. The "default" framing is perspective-bound. C5 is the load-bearing argument.*

**C5. The second law favors extropy.**

If structured systems dissipate gradients more efficiently than unstructured ones, then the second law does not merely tolerate extropy — it selects for it. Not globally, not uniformly, but locally wherever gradients persist. Structure is the fastest path to equilibrium, and the second law favors the fastest path.

The empirical pattern: stars dissipate nuclear gradients more efficiently than diffuse hydrogen. Ecosystems dissipate solar gradients more efficiently than bare rock. Civilization dissipates energy gradients more efficiently than individual organisms. Each level of structure opens access to gradients the previous level could not reach. The sun's nuclear energy is inaccessible to biology until photosynthesis compresses it into chemical form. Fossil energy is inaccessible to biology until technology compresses it into mechanical form. Each compression step unlocks a new gradient, and the new gradient drives new compression.

This is not negentropy fighting entropy from below. It is entropy pulling structure into existence from above. The second law does not want equilibrium slowly. It wants equilibrium *fast*. Structure is how it gets there faster. Extropy is thermodynamically favored — not despite the second law, but because of it.

The production is dual: the same gradient produces both entropy and extropy in the same event. But the directionality is asymmetric. Entropy does not need extropy — the gradient will dissipate either way, through diffusion if nothing else. Extropy needs entropy — structure exists because it is the faster dissipation pathway, and faster pathways are selected. The relationship is genuinely asymmetric in the direction of the drive, even while the production event is one event with two faces.

This asymmetry is appropriate. The duality holds at the level of production: wherever a gradient exists, both terms are produced. But the duality should not be forced into the directional relationship. The second law provides the pressure. Extropy is the response. Acknowledging this does not collapse the framework back into "entropy wins, structure loses." It says: the drive toward equilibrium *produces* structure as its most efficient expression, and structure, once produced, degrades — completing the oscillation. The pendulum swings because of gravity. The kinetic energy does not cause gravity. Asymmetry in the driver is compatible with duality in the motion.

The e/acc insight gets this right: structure accelerates entropy production, so the universe selects for structure. What the derivation adds: the selection is not entropy *using* structure as an instrument. The selection is the gradient expressing itself through compression and dissipation simultaneously. The compression that produces structure and the dissipation that produces entropy are the same event. But within that event, the directional pressure runs one way: toward faster dissipation, which means toward tighter compression, which means toward increasing extropy.

The consequence for scale-directionality: if each level of structure dissipates gradients more efficiently than the last, then the second law provides a thermodynamic drive toward increasing complexity. Not a guarantee — the ladder has rungs that break, transitions that fail, local reversals that collapse. But a bias. The universe does not randomly produce structure. It produces structure preferentially, because structure is how gradients dissipate fastest. The direction of increasing complexity is not an accident. It is the shape of the second law when it acts through compression rather than mere diffusion.

This does not make the ladder inevitable. The bias is real but not absolute. A gradient can dissipate without producing new structure. A structure can degrade without being replaced by a more complex one. The directionality is statistical, not deterministic. But statistics with a bias are not a random walk. They are a driven process with noise. The ladder may have missing rungs. But the rungs that exist point the same way: toward more efficient dissipation, which means toward tighter compression, which means toward increasing extropy.

*Production is dual. Directionality is asymmetric. The second law drives. Extropy is the response. The pendulum swings because of gravity.*

**C6. When the chain breaks.**

The derivation specifies conditions under which extropy should not arise. Three failure modes:

*Gradient without selective differentiation:* all accessible trajectories dissipate equally, no pattern is preferentially stabilized. The gradient relaxes through diffusion alone. No compression, no extropy.

*Selective conditions without retainable invariants:* forcing is too irregular, too rapid, or too noisy for any feature to persist across relevant perturbations. Transient structure appears and collapses before becoming successor-effective.

*Candidate invariants without a stability window:* structure forms but constraints are too weak or perturbations too strong. The compression never stabilizes.

These null cases make the framework discriminative. Extropy does not arise whenever a gradient exists. It arises when a gradient produces active constraints that selectively stabilize features persisting long enough to condition successor dynamics. Missing any link breaks the chain.

*The derivation predicts its own failure modes. Where the chain breaks, no extropy.*

## IV. Differentiations

Extropy is not:

**Negentropy.** Schrödinger's negentropy (1944) is the negation of entropy — life extracts negative entropy from its environment. Extropy is not the opposite of entropy but its co-product. Negentropy implies opposition: life fights entropy and eventually loses. Co-production implies duality: entropy and extropy are generated by the same engine and die together (T7, T8).

**Dissipative structure.** Prigogine's dissipative structures (1947) name the presence of pattern under throughput. Extropy isolates the retained invariant organization insofar as it is inherited by later dynamics as effective condition. A convection roll is a dissipative structure. The way its pattern constrains subsequent flow dynamics is extropy.

**An order parameter.** Order parameters (Haken, 1977) are variables of description that summarize collective macroscopic organization. Extropy is not the parameter itself but the retained organizational product whose stability and successor-effectiveness the parameter may track.

**Memory.** Memory is any dependence of present behavior on past states. The category is too broad. Some memory is ephemeral, local, or causally inert. Extropy denotes a narrower class: retained invariants produced under active constraint that remain consequential for broader successor dynamics.

**Path dependence.** Path dependence names historical sensitivity — outcomes depend on contingent prior sequences. Extropy seeks to identify the retained organizational residue *through which* that dependence is carried forward. Path dependence can exist without isolating what structure has been retained or how it conditions later dynamics. Extropy isolates both.

The gain is not replacing these concepts but isolating a specific explanatory role that cuts across them: the production of retained, successor-effective invariants under active constraint.

## V. Status

The essay's contribution is a **reordering of dependence**: gradients as active constraints, structure formation as selective retention under constraint, extropy as the retained successor-effective product.

The definitions are precise. The derivations follow from the definitions. The strongest claims — entropy production produces extropy (T5), co-production from gradients, co-extensiveness (T8), the heat death reading (T9), choice = abstraction (T10) — are consequences of the definitions and the gradient-constraint identification, not independent assertions.

The gradient-constraint identification is where the risk lies. If D4 (gradient) and D2 (constraint) are defined correctly, and if active constraints are indeed gradient-expressions (T4), the rest follows. If the identification is rejected, the rejection must specify which gradient does not constrain, or which active constraint does not arise from a gradient. Background constraints (conservation laws, fundamental constants) are explicitly excluded — they are the stage, not the action.

The method does not prove the duality is real. It defines terms precisely enough that the duality falls out of the definitions. If the definitions are accepted, the duality is accepted. If the definitions are rejected, the rejection must specify which definition fails and why. That is the strength of the Da Xue method: it forces disagreement to be precise.

The framework is empirically grounded but not yet quantitatively operationalized. A concrete test path: for a system such as Rayleigh-Bénard convection, define a fine-grained flow state $S_t$, a coarse-grained retained descriptor $T_t$, a successor-relevant variable $Y_{t+\Delta}$ (e.g., future transport efficiency), and a dissipation proxy $D_t$. The framework predicts that, across onset and control-parameter variation, the post-threshold regime should show a structured increase in $I(T_t; Y_{t+\Delta})$ relative to retained complexity, and that this increase should relate to $D_t$ in a tradeoff-like geometry. This does not establish a universal metric. It converts the framework into a testable claim.

## VI. Prior Work

**Schrödinger (1944)** introduced *negentropy*: life maintains order by extracting negative entropy from its environment. Brillouin (1950s) formalized this as information. The present derivation agrees that structure feeds on entropy gradients but reframes the relationship: extropy is not the negation of entropy (Schrödinger's framing) but its co-product — what the engine produces as its other face. The difference is not merely verbal. Negentropy implies opposition: life fights entropy and will eventually lose. Co-production implies duality: entropy and extropy are generated by the same engine and die together.

**Prigogine (1947, 1978)** showed that non-equilibrium systems self-organize into dissipative structures — ordered configurations that maximize entropy production. The present derivation agrees and extends: the structure that dissipates most efficiently is the structure that compresses most effectively (C2). Prigogine's maximization of dissipation and Friston's (2006) minimization of free energy are the same event described from outside and inside the compression loop.

**Landauer (1961)** established that information erasure is dissipative: clearing a bit has a minimum thermodynamic cost of $kT \ln 2$. Bennett (1973, 1982) showed that computation can in principle be thermodynamically reversible if no information is erased. This appears to challenge the compression-dissipation identity: if compression can be done reversibly, it need not produce entropy. The derivation's response: the relevant compression is always that of a physical system embedded in a non-equilibrium environment. Abstract reversible computation is a limiting case (zero gradient, zero compression). Where gradients exist, compression is thermodynamically costly and dissipation is inextricable from structuring.

**Kauffman (1993, 2000)** proposed the adjacent possible: biological systems expand into new possibility spaces, creating structures that were not previously accessible. The present framework accommodates this: compression extracts invariants from current conditions, and the invariants thereby revealed open new regions of configuration space. The pendulum swings into territory it has not visited before.

**Haken (1977)** identified order parameters in synergetics: low-dimensional variables that govern macroscopic behavior in non-equilibrium systems. The present derivation identifies order parameters as invariants (D6) and their emergence as compression (D5). The formalisms converge.

**Jaynes (1957)** showed that statistical mechanics can be derived from information-theoretic principles: entropy is maximized subject to constraints representing what an observer knows. The present derivation notes (C4) that this observer-relativity concerns magnitude, not direction. The arrow of entropy increase is robust across all consistent coarse-grainings. Jaynes' insight is real but does not undermine the accumulation asymmetry, which is a directional claim.

**Rovelli (1996, 2021)** proposed the relational interpretation of quantum mechanics: quantum properties are not intrinsic to systems but exist only relative to other systems. The present derivation uses this (C4) to reinforce the point that physics is done from within the system — there is no external perspective from which to measure "global entropy." Combined with special relativity's frame-dependent simultaneity, this challenges the "universe running down" picture as a global projection. But C4 is honest about its limits: this is a philosophical challenge to the framing of "default vs. exception," not a physical refutation of the accumulation asymmetry. The physical argument is C5.

**England (2013)** proposed dissipative adaptation: self-replication emerges because self-replicating structures dissipate energy more efficiently than non-replicating ones. The e/acc movement (Jezos/Verdon, 2022) extends this into a civilizational claim: structure accelerates entropy production, therefore the universe selects for increasing complexity. The present derivation agrees with the directionality but reframes the mechanism: the selection is not entropy *using* structure as an instrument. The selection is the gradient expressing itself through compression (extropy) and dissipation (entropy) simultaneously (C5). The asymmetry in the e/acc framing — structure serves entropy — is replaced by the duality: the same event produces both terms.
