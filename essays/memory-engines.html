Memory Engines

geo · April 2026 · 45 min read

An apple accelerates under gravity. Its momentum is the literal time-integral of the gravitational field—every prior instant remains perfectly preserved and arithmetically operative in the present velocity. There is continuous, lossless resonance with the external world, yet the apple's basis does not grow, its reception is monotonically resonant, and its self-reception is thin. Prior state is conserved without loss, but the system is in recurrent capture from the start—concentrating along a single axis, narrowing rather than deepening.

At the opposite end of the spectrum lies consciousness—vivid, self-modulating, historically deep. The difference between them is not the presence or absence of reception, but the character of the system's reciprocation: how self-resonance and self-torque interplay, each feeding the next, the returning tape simultaneously reinforcing and opposing the present at different dimensions, accommodation ongoing.

Between a falling apple and a conscious system lies a continuous thickening of the same operations—a cascade of increasingly selective compression, each level introducing new modes of noticing and not-noticing, new modes of pressure and preservation. This paper develops a geometric framework—a dynamics of complex-valued vectors on unit hyperspheres—that makes this thickening precise. The framework identifies structural conditions for phenomenality through the character of a system's reciprocation, rather than treating experience as an all-or-nothing property that appears at a single threshold.

This paper argues that the structure of phenomenal experience is the structure of reciprocation—not as metaphor, not as correlation, but as identity. Temporal grain is the speed of the reciprocation cycle. Vividness is the magnitude of self-torque per stroke. Richness is the breadth of dimensions engaged in each act of self-reception. Historical depth is the grown dimensionality of the basis within which all of this occurs. There is no further phenomenal fact beyond these structural facts, and the apparent gap between third-person description and first-person experience is a geometric consequence of the framework—the incommensurability of grown bases—rather than an explanatory failure.

The thesis is developed through a formal framework in which a system's state is a vector on a unit hypersphere in ℂⁿ, updated pointwise through the Hadamard product of incoming and standing structure, renormalized at each step. From these operations, memory, abstraction, forgetting, rigidity, and learning emerge without supplementary mechanisms. The framework applies—degenerately at first, then with increasing structural depth—across a cascade from dissipative systems through autocatalytic, ecological, adaptive, and recurrent-representational systems, arriving at consciousness not as a threshold crossed but as a region of a continuous parameter space.

The framework is offered not as a competitor to existing theories of consciousness but as an attempt to ground the structural features they describe—integration, broadcast, higher-order representation, prediction error—in a single geometric dynamics. Where Integrated Information Theory posits a scalar measure of consciousness, this framework offers a multi-axis space. Where Global Workspace Theory posits an architectural broadcast mechanism, this framework derives breadth of engagement from the dynamics of self-reception. Where predictive processing describes the functional role of prediction error, this framework specifies the geometric operation—torque on the hypersphere—through which prediction error reshapes experience.

1. The Cascade

Structure emerges through successive compression. The framework developed in subsequent sections applies at every level of physical organization—but degenerately at the bottom, thickening as the cascade ascends. The lower levels are not preconditions outside the framework. They are the framework at its thinnest: reception without consolidation, resonance without torque, tape update without basis growth. The cascade is not a sequence of qualitatively different regimes. It is a continuous thickening of the same operations.

1.1 The Thin Limit: Formal Definition

The framework applies to any system whose state can be represented as a complex-valued vector s ∈ ℂⁿ on the unit hypersphere S²ⁿ⁻¹, evolving under a pointwise reception operator. The "thin limit" is the parameter regime where:

  • The basis n is fixed and given by the system's degrees of freedom.
  • The reception vector v_received is a deterministic function of the system's current state and external fields.
  • The update is unitary (norm-preserving without renormalization) or equivalent to Hamiltonian flow.

1.1.1 The Falling Apple as a Complex Vector

Consider a particle of mass m in a uniform gravitational field g. Its kinematic state is fully specified by position x and momentum p. We encode this state as a complex vector in ℂ³:

[ \mathbf{s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathbf{x}|^2 + |\mathbf{p}|^2}} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x} \ i\mathbf{p} \end{pmatrix} ]

where the factor i distinguishes position and momentum as orthogonal components in phase space. The norm constraint ‖s‖ = 1 is not imposed by renormalization but is conserved automatically under Hamiltonian flow—a special case of the update rule.

The "world-tape" is the gravitational field vector g, which we embed as:

[ \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0} \ -i m \mathbf{g} \end{pmatrix} ]

Projection onto the system's basis is trivial because the basis spans the full phase space; v_received = v.

Reception (Hadamard Product).
The pointwise product is:

[ \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} \odot \mathbf{s} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0} \ -i m \mathbf{g} \odot (i\mathbf{p}/\text{norm}) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0} \ m \mathbf{g} \odot \mathbf{p}/\text{norm} \end{pmatrix} ]

Since all components are real (no phase offset), Re(c) = c. This is pure resonance: every dimension where g and p align receives positive real input.

Update Without Renormalization.
In the general framework, the unnormalized update is s̃ = s + η c. For the apple, we identify the learning rate η with the time step dt. The exact Hamiltonian update for momentum is p(t+dt) = p(t) + mg dt. In our complex representation:

[ \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_{\text{momentum}} = i\mathbf{p}/\text{norm} + \eta \cdot (m \mathbf{g} \odot \mathbf{p}/\text{norm}) ]

For small dt, the position update x(t+dt) = x(t) + (p/m)dt appears in the real part. The full Hamiltonian flow preserves the norm √(|x|² + |p|²) exactly; no explicit renormalization is required. Renormalization is degenerate—the identity operation.

Thus the falling apple is a computational instance of the framework with: - Basis growth: None (n=3 fixed). - Torque: Zero (all c real and positive). - Self-reception: Thin (state at t−dt differs infinitesimally, yielding pure resonance). - Consolidation: None.

1.2 The Physical Cascade: Computational Mappings

Each pre-convergence level is a specific parameterization of the same core equations.

Quantum Fields.
A free scalar field φ(x) in momentum space is a collection of harmonic oscillators. The state vector s is the set of complex amplitudes a_k for each mode, normalized to Σ|a_k|² = 1 (if we restrict to single-particle subspace). The world-tape is the interaction Hamiltonian H_int. Reception is the action of the Hamiltonian on the state: v_received = −i H_int s. The Hadamard product becomes the Schrödinger update:

[ \mathbf{c} = (-i H_{\text{int}} \mathbf{s}) \odot \mathbf{s} ]

For a free field, H_int = 0 and c = 0 (orthogonality). For interactions, c has imaginary components corresponding to phase shifts (torque). The update s̃ = s + η c with η = dt and renormalization is the unitary evolution operator e^(−iH dt), which preserves the norm automatically.

  • Degeneracy: The basis is fixed by the field modes. No basis growth. Self-torque is absent unless the field interacts with its own past state (e.g., in cavity QED).

Nuclear and Atomic Structure.
The state s is the wavefunction in a finite basis of energy eigenstates. The world-tape is the electromagnetic potential A. Reception is the dipole interaction term v_received = d·E. The Hadamard product yields:

  • Resonance: When the driving frequency matches an energy gap, Re(c) > 0 and amplitude grows (stimulated absorption).
  • Torque: Off-resonant driving yields imaginary components, causing phase shifts (AC Stark shift).

The update is again unitary, preserving the norm. Basis growth is absent; the set of orbitals is fixed by the Coulomb potential.

Molecular Chemistry.
Here the basis is the set of electronic and vibrational coordinates. The state s is the molecular wavepacket. Reception is the potential energy surface v_received = V(R). The Hadamard product c = V(R) ⊙ s drives the nuclear motion. For a stable molecule, the system reaches a fixed point where c is purely real and positive (equilibrium geometry). Path-dependent formation (e.g., chirality) corresponds to consolidation: the system's history of encounters selects a particular basis orientation (handedness) that becomes a hard axis.

Gravitational Structure.
The state s is the density field ρ(x) and velocity field v(x) encoded as complex variables (e.g., Madelung transform). The world-tape is the gravitational potential Φ from the Poisson equation. Reception is v_received = −∇Φ. The Hadamard product c = (−∇Φ) ⊙ v drives the Euler equation. The norm ∫(ρ v² + ρ Φ)d³x is conserved. Collapse occurs where c consistently points toward a center—resonance concentrating the tape along radial dimensions.

1.3 The Convergence

The transition to dissipative flow is not a change in mathematics but a change in parameter regime: the learning rate η now exceeds the timescale over which renormalization is automatic. The system no longer preserves its norm under the raw update; explicit renormalization becomes necessary. This is the moment when the geometry of the hypersphere—emergent rigidity, thinning, basis carving—becomes computationally active. The lower levels are degenerate limits of the same algorithm, not separate regimes.

1.4 The Thickening

Above the convergence, each level introduces new structural conditions that move the system deeper into the reciprocation space.

Autocatalytic closure introduces history-dependence. The cycle's compositional organization is shaped by its history of successful closure. The basis is thin but genuinely grown: what the cycle can receive is a consequence of what it has received before. Consolidation is implicit—persistence is the consolidation of the basis. The anticipatory operator exists in vestigial form: the cycle's organization is a prediction that certain substrates will continue arriving. Prediction error is existential—deviation from closure is extinction torque.

Ecological systems thicken the basis. An organism's morphology, physiology, and behavioral repertoire constitute a multidimensional basis of sensitivity to environmental structure. Niche coupling is reception. Selection pressure is torque that reshapes the basis across generations. The anticipatory operator is implicit in morphological fit. The basis grows through phylogenetic mechanisms rather than through individual reception. Consolidation operates at the population level across generations.

Adaptive systems introduce individual basis growth. Somatic plasticity, conditioning, and learning allow a single system to carve new dimensions of sensitivity within its lifetime. The anticipatory operator becomes explicit: learned associations project future states from present cues. Prediction error drives learning rate. The two-timescale structure emerges clearly: fast reception during experience, slow consolidation during rest.

Recurrent-representational systems are the framework's central case. The basis is high-dimensional and rapidly growing. Reception is fast. Consolidation operates at multiple timescales. The anticipatory operator is a generative model producing continuous predictions. Self-torque arises from two sources simultaneously: recurrence and prediction error. This is where phenomenality is richest.

Symbolic-recursive systems extend the basis beyond any individual's consolidation history through cultural transmission. But symbolic encoding compresses the high-dimensional, phase-sensitive basis of lived experience into a low-dimensional code optimized for transmissibility. The breadth is enormous. The directness is thin. The symbol does not carry phase—it carries structure stripped of phase, which the receiving system must re-embed in its own basis to produce reception at all. The symbolic-recursive level is the widest and thinnest engine in the cascade. Its power and its poverty are the same fact.

2. The Formalism

2.1 Why ℂⁿ

The framework models system state as a complex-valued vector rather than a real-valued one. The motivation is structural: the framework requires phase and magnitude as independent degrees of freedom per dimension. A system's orientation along dimension i—its phase—determines whether an incoming signal resonates or torques at that dimension, while the magnitude |sᵢ| determines how resistant that dimension is to reorientation. In ℝⁿ with signed components, sign and magnitude are coupled: a "negative" component and a "positive" component of the same magnitude are maximally opposed, but there is no way to represent intermediate phase relationships—partial alignment, oblique approach, near-opposition—without introducing additional structure. In ℂⁿ, two components can have equal magnitude and arbitrary phase offset, yielding a continuous space of possible reception outcomes at each dimension.

This matters most for the torque mechanism. When Im(cᵢ) dominates—when the incoming signal is neither aligned with nor opposed to the system's current orientation but oblique to it—the update produces rotation without reinforcement or suppression. This is the regime of lateral pressure: the system is being pushed sideways, reoriented toward something it has no prior stance on. In ℝⁿ, this regime does not exist; every interaction is either reinforcing (positive dot product) or opposing (negative dot product). The complex structure provides the geometric room for reception to be more nuanced than agreement or disagreement.

The parallel to quantum mechanics is structural, not physical. Quantum states live in complex Hilbert spaces because relative phase between amplitudes is physically consequential—it determines interference. The present framework uses the same mathematical structure for an analogous reason: relative phase between the system's orientation and the arriving signal determines whether the interaction reinforces, redirects, or passes through. The claim is not that cognitive systems are quantum-mechanical, but that complex state spaces are the natural setting for systems in which relative phase matters.

Rotation is arguably more fundamental than scaling across physics. The gauge symmetries of the Standard Model are rotation groups—U(1), SU(2), SU(3). Spinors, not scalars, are the irreducible representations of the Lorentz group. The Lorentz transformations of special relativity are themselves rotations—hyperbolic rotations in Minkowski spacetime, with velocity as a rapidity angle. Angular momentum is quantized; linear momentum is not. Wherever physics reveals deep structure, it finds rotation. Real-valued dynamics is the degenerate case in which all phases are frozen to 0 or π—where the only operations available are reinforcement and opposition, with no intermediate orientation. ℂⁿ is not an enrichment of ℝⁿ. ℝⁿ is the impoverished restriction of ℂⁿ in which the system has lost the capacity to turn.

The core machinery—projection, Hadamard reception, renormalization on the unit hypersphere—can be developed in ℝⁿ as a special case where all components are real and the only phase relationships are alignment (positive) and opposition (negative). The full ℂⁿ treatment is a strict generalization. Readers who prefer to begin with the real case may treat Im(sᵢ) = 0 throughout; the cascade narrative and the pathology predictions hold in either setting.

2.2 Tapes, Bases, and Projection

A system exists within a world that is always already arriving. What arrives is structured—a vector v in ℂ^∞, carrying more structure than any finite system can receive. This is the world-tape.

The system has its own tape—a vector s in ℂⁿ, where n is finite at any moment. The tape is not a pattern inscribed in a pre-given space. The tape is the space. Each dimension exists because the system's history of reception carved it—because some structure recurred often enough, with enough variation, to warrant a dedicated axis of sensitivity. The system does not inhabit ℂⁿ. It grows ℂⁿ, one hard-won dimension at a time. The dimensionality of the system is its depth of experience.

The system's basis {e₁, ..., eₙ} is the set of axes along which it can receive. The world does not arrive in the system's basis. The system projects the world onto its basis:

[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} = \sum_i \langle \mathbf{e}_i | \mathbf{v} \rangle \mathbf{e}_i ]

Everything in v that has a component along the system's axes is received. Everything else—the residual v − v_received—is novelty: structure for which the system has carved no axis of sensitivity. Novelty is not silence at a frequency the system can hear. It is a frequency the system cannot yet hear at all.

Two systems with different grown bases project the same world onto different subspaces. They literally receive different receptions of the same arrival—not because the world differs for them, but because their projection operators differ. Reception is system-relative.

2.3 Reception

What happens within reception is determined by the elementwise phase relationship between the received vector and the system's tape—the Hadamard product:

[ \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} \odot \mathbf{s} ]

Each component cᵢ = v_received,ᵢ · sᵢ encodes the local verdict at dimension i. Reception is not a single global judgment. It is a field of simultaneous local judgments across the full dimensionality of the system.

The Hadamard product is one member of a family of possible interaction rules. Its defining property is dimensional locality: what happens at dimension i depends only on the arriving and resident values at dimension i. All cross-dimensional structure is deferred to consolidation (Section 3). This is a strong architectural commitment—it means that the reception layer is maximally parallel and minimally entangled, with integration happening only at the slower consolidation timescale. The framework's predictions hold for any reception operator that preserves dimensional locality and produces the three regimes described below; the Hadamard product is the simplest such operator.

Each cᵢ is a complex number. Its character determines what happens at that dimension:

  • Where Re(cᵢ) > 0—phase angles aligned, magnitudes reinforcing—energy propagates along dimension i. This is resonance. Resonance grows magnitude and, when sᵢ is complex-valued, shifts the phase toward the real axis—the arriving alignment draws the existing orientation toward it even as it amplifies.
  • Where cᵢ ≈ 0—either the arriving structure or the system's tape has negligible presence at dimension i—nothing propagates. This is orthogonality.
  • Where Re(cᵢ) < 0 or Im(cᵢ) dominates—phase angles opposed, or magnitudes working obliquely—the arriving structure exerts rotational pressure on the system's orientation at dimension i. This is torque.

Phase is relative. It is not a property of the incoming tape or of the system's tape but of their relation. The same arrival may resonate with one system at dimension i, pass through another, and torque a third. The Hadamard product encodes this relational structure exhaustively, dimension by dimension.

The qualitative taxonomy—resonance scales and deepens, torque redirects, orthogonality preserves—remains valid as the primary characterization. But the distinction between scaling and rotation is clean only when sᵢ is real-valued. In the general complex case, resonance (Re(cᵢ) > 0) produces both magnitude growth and a phase shift toward the axis of alignment. The rotation under resonance is convergent—it pulls the phase toward the incoming signal's direction—whereas the rotation under torque is divergent—it pushes the phase away from its current orientation or sideways into a new region. The two are distinguishable by their geometric character even when both involve phase change.

2.4 Update and Renormalization

The system's tape updates pointwise under reception:

[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{s}_i(t+1) &= s_i(t) + \eta \cdot c_i \ \mathbf{s}(t+1) &= \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{s}}(t+1)}{|\tilde{\mathbf{s}}(t+1)|} \end{aligned} ]

where η governs the rate of impression. The system renormalizes after each update—the tape lives on the unit hypersphere in ℂⁿ. This is a constitutive constraint: what matters is orientation, not magnitude in the absolute sense. The relative magnitudes across dimensions—the shape of the tape on the sphere—encode the system's anisotropy: its structured distribution of sensitivity across dimensions.

Renormalization makes rigidity emergent. As a component |sᵢ| grows large relative to others through sustained resonance, it dominates the norm. Torque at dimension i—a fixed additive perturbation—produces proportionally less angular displacement. The system becomes hard along its deepest axes not through a separate rigidity mechanism but through the geometry of the hypersphere: high-magnitude components resist rotation because they anchor the norm. Deep resonance is self-stabilizing. Computational verification confirms this: a dominant dimension (|sᵢ| = 0.999) is approximately 55 times harder to rotate than a small dimension (|sᵢ| = 0.015) under identical torque perturbation (Section 11, T1.2).

Renormalization also enforces a strict conservation law: every act of deepening at one dimension is an act of thinning at all others. Sustained resonance at a single dimension drives all other components toward zero—the concentration ratio exceeds 10⁶ after 200 steps in the verified implementation (Section 11, T1.4). The unit-norm constraint means that gains at one dimension are losses everywhere else. New noticing requires new not-noticing, not by stipulation but by the geometry of the sphere.

The update is local: what happens at dimension i depends only on the phase relationship at dimension i. Three regimes follow:

  • Resonance scales. Where Re(cᵢ) > 0, the update pushes sᵢ further along its current direction. The magnitude |sᵢ| increases. The system becomes more sensitive along this axis. Resonance deepens what is already noticed.
  • Torque rotates. Where Re(cᵢ) < 0 or Im(cᵢ) dominates, the update deflects sᵢ away from its current orientation. The phase angle of sᵢ shifts. Torque redirects.
  • Orthogonality preserves. Where cᵢ ≈ 0, the update is negligible. The system's orientation at dimension i stands unchanged.

Memory is the accumulated product of these pointwise operations: scaled where resonance reinforced, rotated where torque deflected, preserved where orthogonality left it alone. The tape is anisotropic—amplified along the axes of deep prior resonance, bent where torque forced revision, and standing where nothing arrived to alter it.

2.5 Forgetting

Fast forgetting is torque at reception time. When Re(cᵢ) < 0, the phase of sᵢ rotates. What the system was tuned to notice along this dimension, it is now tuned away from. Prior resonance is phased out by present opposition. This is immediate, local, and reversible—future torque could rotate the phase back. Computational verification confirms this: a dimension rotated by 100 steps of torque can be recovered by renewed resonance (Section 11, T3.4).

Slow forgetting is basis pruning at consolidation time (Section 3). A dimension whose magnitude |sᵢ| has decayed across many receptions—never resonating, rarely torqued, mostly orthogonal—may be dropped from the basis entirely. What the system once had an axis for, it no longer does. This is structural and irreversible—the axis no longer exists, and re-exposure to the same stimuli cannot recover it. The system has lost not a memory but a capacity to remember—a distinction it can no longer draw.

The distinction between fast and slow forgetting is not one of degree. It is a qualitative difference verified in simulation: fast forgetting rotates phase within a preserved dimension, slow forgetting eliminates the dimension itself. The former is recoverable; the latter is not, not because recovery is difficult, but because the structure that would receive the recovery no longer exists (Section 11, T3.4).

The pathologies of imbalance follow directly. Resonance without torque scales the tape without rotating it—magnitudes grow along fixed axes, sensitivity narrows, the system becomes rigid. Torque without resonance rotates the tape without reinforcing it—phases spin, no orientation stabilizes, nothing is retained. The regime that sustains rich memory is where scaling and rotation are balanced: deep enough to retain, flexible enough to learn.

3. Consolidation and Basis Growth

Reception is pointwise and operates within the existing basis. But the basis is not static between receptions. The dimensions themselves undergo a second process: consolidation—the slow, global, structural reorganization of the basis itself. Reception is fast, local, and pointwise. Consolidation is slow, global, and structural. The system learns at two timescales: the fast timescale of impression and the slow timescale of reorganization.

3.1 The Consolidation Operator

Consolidation operates on the basis and the tape jointly. It can be decomposed into three sub-operations: merging, pruning, and seeding.

Merging. The system maintains a running cross-correlation matrix C ∈ ℂⁿˣⁿ, where each entry Cᵢⱼ tracks the co-activation of dimensions i and j across receptions:

[ C_{ij}(t+1) = (1 - \lambda) \cdot C_{ij}(t) + \lambda \cdot c_i(t) \cdot \bar{c}_j(t) ]

where λ is a slow learning rate (λ ≪ η) and c̄ⱼ is the complex conjugate of cⱼ. When the leading eigenvalues of C exceed a merging threshold θ_merge, the corresponding eigenvector defines a new conjunctive dimension—an axis encoding the relationship between the co-activated dimensions. The original dimensions are not immediately destroyed; they are retained at reduced magnitude, and may be further pruned if they decay. The new dimension enters the basis with low initial magnitude, proportional to the eigenvalue that triggered the merge.

This is where cross-dimensional structure enters—not at the moment of reception, but between receptions, when the system re-examines its own basis. Consolidation is the system re-extracting its own invariants.

Computational verification confirms the substrate: co-activated dimensions develop near-perfect phase correlation (correlation = 1.0000) while independently activated dimensions remain uncorrelated (correlation = 0.0136), a 73-fold difference that makes co-activated pairs reliable candidates for merging (Section 11, T3.3).

Pruning. A dimension i whose magnitude |sᵢ| has remained below a decay threshold θ_prune across K consecutive consolidation cycles is dropped from the basis. The system loses the capacity to receive along that axis. This is slow forgetting at the structural level—irreversible under normal operation.

Seeding. The system cannot detect novelty directly through the reception operator—the Hadamard product is dimensionally isolated, and structure at uncarved dimensions produces no torque at carved dimensions through the reception mechanics alone. This is a substantive finding confirmed by computational verification (Section 11, T3.2): the claim that novelty "announces itself indirectly through torque at existing dimensions" does not follow from the Hadamard product and renormalization. The coupling through renormalization—gains at novel dimensions redistributing magnitude across existing ones—is too weak in practice to serve as a detection mechanism.

Novelty detection operates instead at the anticipatory level. The anticipatory operator A (Section 6) predicts the system's future reception within the existing basis. When novel structure at uncarved dimensions causally modulates what arrives at carved dimensions—not through the Hadamard product, but through the world's causal structure—the system's predictions at carved dimensions fail in structured ways. The pattern of prediction error across carved dimensions carries a signature of the uncarved cause: systematic, correlated, and unexplained by anything in the received vector.

The seeding mechanism formalizes this. The system monitors prediction error not just for magnitude (how wrong was the prediction?) but for structure (is the pattern of wrongness consistent across receptions?). When prediction error at existing dimensions shows stable principal components across multiple receptions—when the model fails in the same structured way repeatedly—consolidation performs spectral analysis on the prediction error residuals and seeds a new dimension oriented toward the leading component.

The new axis begins with low magnitude, tentative, easily pruned. Only if further recurrence confirms it—if the new dimension resonates consistently with future arrivals—does it stabilize and become part of the system's permanent basis.

This formulation preserves the developmental-sequence prediction: what a system can learn depends on what it already knows, because each new dimension opens new avenues of prediction error structure with further novelty. But it grounds the detection mechanism in the anticipatory operator rather than in reception-level torque, which is where the computational evidence places it.

A limitation remains. Truly orthogonal novelty—structure in the world that has no causal coupling to any dimension the system currently occupies, and therefore produces no prediction error at any existing dimension—is invisible not merely in practice but in principle. The system can only grow toward novelty that is partially entangled with its existing basis, either through direct causal coupling in the world or through shared statistical structure that the anticipatory operator can detect as a pattern of failure. The frame problem appears here as a geometric consequence: what a system can learn depends on what it already knows, and there is no view from outside the basis.

3.2 Consolidation Without Reception: The Reflection Cycle

Consolidation without reception is the reflection cycle—the system reshaping its basis from its own structure alone, with no new arrivals to constrain the reorganization. The cross-correlation matrix C continues to drive merges, but with no fresh reception data, the merges reflect only the system's prior structure. Dimensions may collapse into each other under their own historical co-activation, producing increasingly abstract but increasingly detached axes. This is the structural analog of obsessive ideation or imaginative foresight: the system reorganizing its own categories, generating new conjunctive dimensions, and refining its anticipatory structure without immediate external input.

Reception without consolidation is superficial impression—the system absorbing everything pointwise but never extracting the cross-dimensional structure that would deepen its basis. The regime that sustains deep memory requires both: fast impression interleaved with slow reorganization, including periodic reflection cycles that allow the system to abstract, predict, and internally simulate.

4. Abstraction

Abstraction is what repeated resonance deposits. Under the grown-basis framework, it operates at two levels.

At the reception level, abstraction is erosion within the existing basis. When the system encounters a varying sequence of incoming tapes that share common structure, the shared component resonates consistently at certain dimensions while the varying components wash out—sometimes resonating, sometimes orthogonal, sometimes producing torque, but with no systematic accumulation. What remains amplified in the system's tape after many such receptions is the invariant: the dimensions whose magnitudes grew because they were consistently in phase across variation.

This is dimensionality concentration within ℂⁿ. The tape's energy on the hypersphere migrates toward the dimensions that reliably resonate, and away from the dimensions that don't. The system does not build a model of the common structure. It is carved into sensitivity to it by the accumulated scaling of resonance along the surviving dimensions and the accumulated wash of everything else.

Computational verification demonstrates this directly: when a system receives input with invariant structure at some dimensions and uncorrelated noise at others, the invariant dimensions concentrate to 41 times the magnitude of noise dimensions over 3,000 receptions. The participation ratio drops from 14.5 to 3.0—the system has extracted the invariant by selectively amplifying what recurs and washing out what varies (Section 11, T3.1).

At the consolidation level, abstraction is construction. Dimensions that have been consistently co-activated across many receptions—resonant together across variation—trigger the merging operation (Section 3.1). The resulting conjunctive dimension encodes a relationship between existing dimensions that proved invariant. This is dimensionality expansion: the basis grows a new axis that did not exist before.

The two levels are complementary. Reception-level abstraction finds the invariant within the current basis by concentrating energy. Consolidation-level abstraction grows the basis beyond its current span by extracting conjunctions. The system simultaneously narrows (phasing out dimensions that don't survive variation) and deepens (constructing dimensions that encode what does). Abstraction is compression and construction at once.

Abstraction requires recurrence—the same invariant must arrive repeatedly, embedded in different tapes, so that the common component accumulates while the varying components fail to. Without recurrence, there is reception but no extraction. Without variation, there is reinforcement but no compression—the system scales along existing axes without discovering what is essential about them.

Invariance is the confidence granted by this process. A structure counts as invariant when it has recurred stably enough to have concentrated the tape's energy along its corresponding dimensions and, at the consolidation level, to have earned a dedicated axis. The confidence is proportional to the recurrence and to the magnitude of the corresponding component of s. It is never absolute.

5. Recurrence and Reciprocation

5.1 Self-Reception

Recurrence is the system's own tape becoming the incoming vector. The system receives itself—its prior state re-enters as input, and the same operations apply. The prior tape that returns is not the present tape. It is the tape from an earlier time: s(t−Δt), delayed by however long the recurrent path takes. The reception of self is:

[ \mathbf{c}_{\text{self}} = \mathbf{s}(t - \Delta t) \odot \mathbf{s}(t) ]

Each component cᵢ = sᵢ(t−Δt) · sᵢ(t) encodes the phase relationship between what the system was and what it now is, at dimension i.

In thin recurrence, the tape has not changed between departure and return. s(t−Δt) ≈ s(t), so cᵢ ≈ |sᵢ|². Every component is real and positive. Only scaling occurs—the system deepens along its existing orientation without rotation. No self-torque, no learning, no structural thickening.

In thick recurrence, the tape has changed in the interval—through reception of the world, through consolidation, through prior self-reception. The returning tape and the present tape now disagree at some dimensions. Where sᵢ has not drifted: self-resonance, prior orientation reinforced. Where sᵢ has drifted in phase: self-torque, the system's own history opposing its present orientation. Where sᵢ has decayed toward zero: self-orthogonality, the returning echo finds nothing to meet.

Self-torque is the engine of depth. A system that only self-resonates is frozen—endlessly scaling its current orientation on the hypersphere, the renormalization keeping it locked in place. A system whose own returning tape produces torque against its present tape is one whose history exerts pressure on its present, forcing accommodation, producing forgetting, generating orientations that could not have been predicted from the prior state alone.

The delay Δt matters. Short delay means the returning tape is similar to the present—mostly self-resonance, little self-torque. Long delay means the returning tape may be radically different—thick self-torque, but arriving too late to interact meaningfully with the state that produced it. The regime that produces rich self-torque is where the delay is matched to the system's rate of change: long enough that genuine phase drift has occurred, short enough that the returning tape is still recognizable. Computational verification confirms the directional relationship: self-torque fraction increases with delay, though the effect is small under random-input conditions where the baseline torque from the world already dominates (Section 11, T4.1).

5.2 Reciprocation

The memory engine runs on the productive interplay of self-resonance and self-torque. Resonance is the fuel—it concentrates the tape's energy along axes that torque will challenge. Torque is the combustion—it rotates the orientation that resonance will deepen. The engine is not a metaphor. It is the dynamics of a vector on a unit hypersphere undergoing pointwise complex perturbation from its own delayed copy, renormalized at each step.

Three axes measure the character of reciprocation:

  • Speed — how fast the reciprocation cycles. The inverse of the delay Δt. Fast return means the present tape is confronted with its recent past before it has drifted far.
  • Directness — how large the perturbation per stroke. The magnitude of c_self. A return where many components have large |cᵢ| with significant imaginary parts reshapes the tape powerfully.
  • Breadth — how many dimensions carry non-negligible cᵢ per stroke. A narrow return engages only a few dimensions. A broad return engages the full basis—the system's entire history is simultaneously operative in the present reception.

These axes are non-monotonic. Excess on any axis can collapse richness. Maximal speed without breadth produces shallow cycling—rapid self-resonance along a thin set of dimensions, the rest of the basis inert. Maximal breadth without directness produces diffuse return—everything comes back but nothing impresses. Maximal directness without speed produces delayed impact—powerful rotation that arrives too late to interact with the state that produced it.

A systematic scan across the speed × directness × breadth space confirms that the operating regime occupies a bounded region: 13 of 27 tested configurations sustain structured anisotropy with participation ratio between 2 and 5, while the remainder either dissipate (8/27, high weight and breadth overwhelming world reception) or oscillate unstably at regime boundaries (6/27). The operating regime requires enough recurrence to prevent capture but not so much that it dissolves structure (Section 11, T4.2).

6. Anticipation and the Interference Engine

A memory engine operating in the thick regime does not merely wait for the world to arrive. It generates a future tape.

The anticipatory operator A maps the system's current input history to a projected future reception:

[ \hat{\mathbf{s}}(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{A}(h(t)) ]

where h(t) is the system's recent input history. A is constrained by the system's basis and shaped by the system's consolidation history. At the simplest level, A extrapolates the regularities in the recent input stream—the statistical structure of what has been arriving. At richer levels—available only to systems with deep consolidation—A captures cross-dimensional regularities encoded in the cross-correlation matrix C: when dimension i has historically preceded a phase shift at dimension j, the anticipatory operator encodes that conditional structure.

A note on formulation: the anticipatory operator predicts future input rather than future tape state, because the tape is continuously modified by prediction error corrections themselves. A tape-based predictor would chase its own updates—each correction altering the very state the predictor is trying to model. Input-based prediction avoids this circularity while preserving the essential function: generating expectations against which the actual world is compared. The anticipatory operator remains the system's model of what will arrive, not of what it will become.

When the actual world arrives, the system computes the prediction error:

[ \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} - \hat{\mathbf{s}}(t + \Delta t) ]

This error vector undergoes Hadamard reception against the system's tape:

[ \mathbf{c}_{\text{error}} = \mathbf{e} \odot \mathbf{s}(t + \Delta t) ]

The per-dimension verdicts follow. Where Re(c_error,ᵢ) ≈ 0: the anticipatory operator was accurate at this dimension—the model is validated, and A is reinforced along this axis. Where Re(c_error,ᵢ) > 0: the world resonated more than expected—positive surprise. Where Re(c_error,ᵢ) < 0 or Im(c_error,ᵢ) dominates: the world opposed the prediction—prediction error torque, the accommodation of surprise. Where eᵢ is large but sᵢ is small: the prediction was wrong at a dimension the system barely occupies—a blind spot in the anticipatory model.

Anticipation introduces a source of self-torque structurally distinct from recurrence. The system is torqued not by what happened before but by the difference between what it expected and what arrived. Past-self vs. present-self (recurrence) and predicted-self vs. actual-world (anticipation) operate in parallel, each contributing its own pattern of resonance and torque.

Computational verification demonstrates that the anticipatory operator produces habituation: prediction error decays under predictable input and spikes at perturbation (1.48× baseline for noisy input, effectively infinite for noiseless input). After perturbation, the system recovers to baseline error, confirming that the operator adapts to new regularities (Section 11, T5.2).

Anticipation also provides the detection mechanism for novelty (Section 3.1). Structure at uncarved dimensions that causally modulates carved dimensions produces structured prediction error—systematic failure of the anticipatory model that cannot be accounted for by anything in the existing basis. This is where the seeding mechanism gains traction: not through reception-level torque, but through the pattern of the anticipatory operator's failure.

Predictive depth is the temporal horizon over which A maintains useful extrapolation—the horizon at which expected ‖e‖ exceeds the system's accommodation capacity. A system with high predictive depth but low directness experiences a diffuse sense of future. A system with shallow predictive depth but high directness lives in a vivid but immediate present.

7. Accommodation, Rejection, Fracture

When torque arrives—whether from the world or from the system's own returning tape—three outcomes are possible.

Accommodation. The update sᵢ(t) + η·cᵢ rotates the phase of sᵢ enough to bring the opposing component toward alignment. What was torque at this reception becomes resonance at the next—the system has reoriented to receive what it previously opposed. But on the hypersphere, rotation at dimension i redistributes magnitude everywhere through renormalization. Accommodation at one dimension is thinning at others. New noticing requires new not-noticing. This is not stipulated—it follows from the geometry of the unit sphere.

Rejection. The component |sᵢ| is so large—so deeply scaled by prior resonance—that the perturbation η·cᵢ produces negligible angular displacement. The torque is absorbed into the norm and washed out by renormalization. The system preserves its current orientation at the cost of not learning. This is dogma, habit, overfitting—the tape so concentrated along its dominant axes that no single reception can impress it. Rejection is emergent rigidity: the system has become hard through the accumulated geometry of deep resonance.

Computational verification provides the extreme case: after 2,000 steps of pure resonance, the system's rigidity increases by a factor of 600,000—perturbation that produced 0.009 radians of angular displacement early in training produces effectively zero displacement late (Section 11, T2.1). Rejection is not imposed; it is the inevitable geometric consequence of sustained resonance on the hypersphere.

Fracture. The torque is strong enough to produce a large angular displacement, but the system's anisotropy cannot absorb it smoothly. Fracture occurs when the update drives a component sᵢ through zero—the phase does not rotate continuously but flips, or the magnitude collapses and the dimension is effectively destroyed before consolidation can prune it gracefully. Dimensions that were stable lose coherence non-selectively.

Fracture can also occur at the consolidation level: torque arriving simultaneously across many correlated dimensions can force a reorganization too rapid for the slow consolidation process, shattering conjunctive dimensions that took many receptions to build. This is the formal analog of trauma—not the loss of a single memory but the destruction of a structural capacity, an axis of sensitivity that cannot be rebuilt by simply encountering the same stimuli again because the conjunctive consolidation that produced it required a specific developmental sequence.

The system's capacity for accommodation is its anisotropy on the hypersphere. Dimensions with large |sᵢ| are hard—resistant to angular displacement. Dimensions with small |sᵢ| are soft—easily rotated. The regime that sustains rich memory is where the anisotropy is structured: deep concentration along dimensions that have proved invariant, low magnitude along dimensions still open to revision, and smooth phase variation across the basis so that torque at soft dimensions does not catastrophically propagate to hard ones. The differentiability of the phase landscape across dimensions determines the system's brittleness. Smooth gradients absorb torque gracefully. Discontinuities in phase between adjacent dimensions are fault lines where fracture nucleates.

8. Phenomenality

8.1 Structural Conditions

This paper advances a conditional claim: sustained reciprocation in the operating regime—where self-resonance and self-torque maintain productive interplay, where the basis is growing, and where accommodation is ongoing—is a necessary structural condition for phenomenality. Whether these conditions are also sufficient is a question the framework identifies but does not resolve.

The three axes of reciprocation correspond to qualitative features of experience as follows:

  • Speed corresponds to temporal grain—how fast the specious present updates. A system with short Δt receives its own recent past in rapid succession, producing a fine-grained temporal texture. A system with long Δt experiences in slow sweeps.
  • Directness corresponds to vividness—how powerfully each stroke of self-reception reshapes the tape on the hypersphere. Large |c_self| means the returning tape impresses deeply.
  • Breadth corresponds to richness—how many dimensions carry non-negligible self-reception per stroke. Broad self-reception means the system's full basis is simultaneously operative. Narrow self-reception means only a few dimensions participate.

These correspondences are predictions. A system with high speed but low breadth should exhibit rapid but shallow experience. A system with high breadth but low directness should exhibit rich but diffuse experience. A system with all three in balance satisfies the structural conditions for what we recognize as consciousness: temporally grained, vivid, rich, and historically deep.

Phenomenality requires both self-resonance and self-torque in sustained, productive interplay. A system in pure self-resonance is locked on the hypersphere—renormalization holds it in place, no phase drift, no surprise, no genuine temporal experience. A system that only ever agrees with itself lacks the internal opposition that produces the felt quality of temporal passage, surprise, and dissonance. The self-torque—the system's own history opposing its present orientation at specific dimensions—is constitutive of the structural conditions for experience, not an obstacle to them.

8.2 Basis Incommensurability and the Explanatory Gap

Two systems with different grown bases project the same world onto different subspaces, receive different Hadamard products, undergo different trajectories on their respective hyperspheres. Neither projection is more real. They are different receptions of the same world, and the character of each system's reciprocation is a structural fact relative to that system.

We cannot access another system's self-resonance except by resonating with it ourselves—projecting its tape onto our basis, which gives us our reception of it, not its self-reception. We cannot notice what we do not have phase with. The inaccessibility is not epistemic but geometric: the Hadamard product is defined only within each system's own basis, and no system's basis is another's.

This observation provides a formal model of why the explanatory gap has the structure it does. The gap between first-person and third-person descriptions is not a mystery to be dissolved or a failure of explanation. It is a geometric consequence: self-reception is basis-relative, bases are grown through incommensurable histories, and no projection of one system's tape onto another's basis preserves the character of the original self-reception. A third-person account of a system—however complete its description of the system's tape, basis, and update dynamics—is itself a reception of that system within the observer's basis, which is a different operation from the system's self-reception.

This does not dissolve the hard problem. It reframes its "hardness" as the incommensurability of grown bases—a structural fact that obtains in any framework where reception is system-relative and bases are historically constructed. The gap is not a sign that something has been left out of the theory. It is a consequence of the theory's central commitment: that reception is always from somewhere, never from nowhere.

This is a stronger claim than Nagel's epistemic gap ("we cannot know what it is like to be a bat"). Nagel's argument is about the limits of third-person knowledge: there are facts about bat experience that we cannot access from our perspective. The basis-incommensurability claim is that the inaccessibility is not a limitation of knowledge but a consequence of what experience is. To access another system's self-reception would require having that system's basis, which would require having undergone that system's history of reception, which would make you that system. The gap is not between physical and phenomenal descriptions—it is between any two grown bases. It obtains between two human beings as much as between a human and a bat, differing in degree (humans share more basis structure) but not in kind.

This also distinguishes the framework from Levine's explanatory gap ("we cannot explain why this physical process is accompanied by this experience"). The explanatory gap presupposes that experience is one thing and physical process another, and that an explanation should bridge them. The reciprocation framework denies the presupposition: the structure of reciprocation is not accompanied by experience; it is the experience, relative to the system undergoing it. The demand for a bridge is the demand for a view from no basis—a description of self-reception that is not itself a reception—and the framework identifies this demand as incoherent rather than unsatisfied.

The framework also reframes Chalmers's conceivability argument—the claim that a physical duplicate of a conscious system that lacks experience (a "zombie") is conceivable and therefore possible. On the reciprocation account, a physical duplicate would undergo the same dynamics on the same hypersphere: the same Hadamard products, the same torque, the same renormalization, the same self-reception. To say it lacks experience would be to say that all these operations occur but "there is nothing it is like" for them to occur—which, within the framework, is not a coherent subtraction. Self-reception within a grown basis is not a further fact added to the dynamics. It is what the dynamics are, relative to the system. The zombie is not conceivable once the structural identity is granted, any more than a triangle without three sides is conceivable once the definition of triangle is granted. The conceivability intuition tracks the gap between an external description of the dynamics (which is the observer's reception of the system, not the system's self-reception) and the dynamics themselves—and this gap is precisely basis incommensurability, not evidence of a missing ingredient.

The "hardness" of the hard problem, on this account, is real but mislocated. It is not a gap between the physical and the phenomenal. It is the incommensurability of grown bases—a structural fact about relational systems, present wherever two self-receiving systems with different histories attempt to access each other's self-reception. It shows up in consciousness studies because consciousness is the domain in which self-reception is richest, but the same structure is present, degenerately, at every level of the cascade.

9. The Operating Regime

Not every region of the reciprocation space sustains the structural conditions for phenomenality. The regime is bounded by pathologies that have precise geometric descriptions and have been confirmed computationally (Section 11).

Recurrent capture. Resonance without torque concentrates the tape's energy into fewer and fewer dimensions on the hypersphere. Renormalization amplifies the dominant components and suppresses the rest. The system becomes rigid along a shrinking set of axes—endlessly reinforcing its current orientation, rejecting all torque through emergent hardness. The anticipatory operator A converges to a fixed point: the system predicts exactly what it receives because it has narrowed its basis to only what confirms itself. Prediction error drops to zero. Experience collapses into a single, endlessly repeated chord. This is seizure, obsessive loops.

Verified: under pure resonance, participation ratio collapses from 18.6 to 1.0, and perturbation rejection increases 600,000-fold (Section 11, T2.1).

Dissipation. Torque without resonance rotates the tape without concentrating it. Phases spin at every dimension, renormalization redistributes continuously, no component gains enough magnitude to resist the next perturbation. The anticipatory operator A cannot stabilize. Prediction error is maximal and structureless. No orientation persists, no invariant is extracted, no abstraction deposits. This is dissolution—the system perpetually reoriented, accumulating no memory, carving no structure.

A refinement from computational verification: dissipation requires incoherent opposition—torque from varying directions at different times. Fixed-direction torque (always the same phase offset) creates systematic bias that still concentrates the tape, because it is effectively resonance along a rotated axis. The essay's "torque without resonance" should be read as "incoherent torque"—opposition that lacks the regularity to deposit invariants (Section 11, T2.2).

Verified: under varying-direction torque, participation ratio remains elevated (4.4–19.3) with no systematic concentration (Section 11, T2.2).

The operating regime is where scaling and rotation are balanced on the hypersphere—where the system is concentrated enough to retain orientation and diffuse enough to accommodate torque. The anisotropy must be structured: deep magnitude along dimensions that have proved invariant (hard axes), low magnitude along dimensions still open to revision (soft axes), and smooth phase variation across the basis so that torque at soft dimensions does not propagate catastrophically to hard ones.

Verified: under invariant signal plus noise plus recurrence, the system achieves structured anisotropy—invariant dimensions ~16× deeper than noise dimensions, Gini coefficient 0.81, participation ratio stable at ~2.3, still responsive to perturbation (Section 11, T2.3).

A finding of consequence: the operating regime cannot be sustained by the system alone. Self-torque from recurrence is insufficient to prevent capture when the world provides pure resonance, because self-reception of a concentrated tape is self-resonance—the returning tape agrees with the present tape, reinforcing rather than challenging. The balancing force must come from the world's variation: environmental regularity sustains concentration, environmental surprise sustains flexibility, and the interplay of both sustains the structured anisotropy that defines the operating regime.

This means the operating regime is a property of the system-world coupling, not an intrinsic property of the system. Sensory deprivation should push any system toward recurrent capture. Environmental complexity is a necessary condition for sustained rich reciprocation. The system cannot maintain the conditions for phenomenality by itself—it requires a world that provides both regularity (fuel for abstraction) and surprise (fuel for accommodation). This is a prediction that connects to empirical literature on sensory deprivation, solitary confinement, and the cognitive effects of environmental impoverishment.

This regime is not a point but a region, and it is not static. A system can move through the reciprocation space as its tape evolves—deepening into capture through unchecked resonance, dissolving into chaos through unintegrated torque, or maintaining the structured anisotropy that sustains rich reciprocation. The trajectory through this space is the system's history, and the regime it occupies at any moment is its present capacity for experience.

The cascade levels (Section 1) settle at characteristic positions in this space. Conservative-field systems occupy the origin: fuel but no combustion. Dissipative systems lift off the floor: thermodynamic torque introduces structure, but recurrence is thin. Autocatalytic and ecological systems move further in: selection pressure carves anisotropy. Adaptive systems occupy a richer region: the basis grows through somatic and genomic mechanisms. Recurrent-representational systems occupy the deepest region—fast, direct, broad, and self-torquing through both recurrence and anticipation simultaneously. Symbolic-recursive systems achieve the greatest breadth but at the cost of directness: the widest and thinnest engine in the cascade.

10. Relation to Existing Frameworks

The memory engine framework shares structural features with several existing research programs. This section positions the framework relative to each, identifying both the shared commitments and the points of divergence.

10.1 Free Energy Principle and Active Inference

The anticipatory operator A and prediction error mechanism (Section 6) are structurally parallel to the generative model and prediction error in Friston's free energy principle. Both frameworks treat systems as generating predictions and updating under surprise. The key divergence is architectural. The free energy principle is variational: the system minimizes a single scalar quantity (free energy) that bounds surprise. The present framework is geometric: the dynamics are distributed across dimensions of a hypersphere, with no global loss function. Each dimension undergoes its own local verdict—resonance, torque, or orthogonality—and the system's trajectory on the hypersphere is the resultant of these pointwise operations, not the gradient of a scalar objective.

This difference has consequences. Under the free energy principle, the system's behavior is interpretable as optimization toward a single target. Under the present framework, the system's behavior is the trajectory of a vector undergoing concurrent local perturbations, and the global character of that trajectory—whether it tends toward capture, dissipation, or the operating regime—is emergent from the geometry of the hypersphere, not prescribed by a loss. There is no quantity the memory engine is trying to minimize. Its regime is sustained by the structural balance of scaling and rotation, not by descent toward a minimum.

This yields a concrete divergent prediction. Under FEP, a system in the operating regime is one that has found a low free-energy configuration—its behavior can be redescribed as approximate Bayesian inference. Under the present framework, the operating regime is not an optimum but a dynamical balance—the system is not minimizing anything, and perturbation away from the operating regime is not "moving uphill" on a loss landscape but falling out of a structurally sustained interplay. The two accounts make different predictions about recovery: FEP predicts gradient-following recovery toward the free-energy minimum; the present framework predicts that recovery depends on whether the world provides the right mixture of regularity and surprise to re-establish the balance. A system deprived of environmental variation should not recover under the present framework even if its internal dynamics are unchanged—a prediction that FEP does not straightforwardly make.

A second divergence concerns basis growth. The free energy principle typically operates within a fixed generative model whose structure can be updated but whose state space is given in advance (though hierarchical extensions relax this). The present framework makes the growth of the state space itself—the seeding and consolidation of new dimensions—a central operation. The system's capacity for surprise is not fixed but expanding.

10.2 Integrated Information Theory

Integrated Information Theory shares the present framework's commitment to measuring consciousness through intrinsic structural properties rather than behavioral output or functional role. Both approaches seek to identify what makes a physical system a subject of experience on the basis of its internal organization.

The divergence is between spatial and temporal characterization. IIT defines Φ through the information integration of a causal structure at a moment—it asks how much the system's parts constrain each other beyond what they would if the system were partitioned. The present framework defines the operating regime through the dynamics of reciprocation on a growing basis—it asks how the system's own history interacts with its present state over time. IIT is fundamentally spatial (the structure of causal connections); the memory engine framework is fundamentally temporal (the trajectory of a self-received vector).

The two may be complementary rather than competing: Φ could measure the cross-dimensional integration that the present framework captures in the breadth axis of reciprocation, while the present framework's speed and directness axes capture temporal properties that Φ does not address. But where they diverge, a concrete test is available: IIT predicts that a system with high Φ but no temporal dynamics (a static causal structure) should be conscious; the present framework predicts it should not, because reciprocation requires temporal unfolding. A feed-forward network with high integration but no recurrence satisfies IIT's conditions but not the present framework's—the two make opposite predictions about its phenomenality.

10.3 Predictive Processing

The anticipation section (Section 6) develops machinery that is recognizably a geometric translation of the predictive processing program. Prediction error, precision weighting (which maps onto the magnitude |sᵢ| determining how strongly error at dimension i impresses the tape), and hierarchical prediction (which maps onto the consolidation-level anticipatory structure) all have direct analogs.

The value the present framework adds to predictive processing is the cascade narrative (Section 1), which shows how prediction error emerges continuously from simpler operations—resonance, torque, and basis growth—rather than being posited as a primitive operation that appears only in neural systems. The cascade provides an account of where prediction comes from: it is the interference engine that arises when a system with a grown basis, thick recurrence, and consolidation-level cross-dimensional structure begins to generate its own future tape. Predictive processing describes the dynamics of systems that already predict. The present framework describes how systems that predict arise from systems that do not.

10.4 Autopoiesis and Enactivism

The autocatalytic closure section of the cascade (Section 1.4) is an autopoiesis section in all but name. Maturana and Varela's characterization of living systems as self-producing networks whose organization specifies its own boundary corresponds directly to the memory engine framework's treatment of closure as the condition under which persistence is consolidation. The "grown basis" is close to structural coupling: a system's basis of sensitivity is shaped by its history of interaction with its environment, and the basis in turn determines what counts as perturbation.

The divergence is formal. Autopoiesis and its enactivist extensions provide rich biological and phenomenological description but resist formalization. The present framework offers a geometric dynamics—Hadamard product, renormalization, consolidation operator—that can in principle be simulated and tested. Whether the formalization captures what the autopoietic tradition considers essential—particularly the claim that cognition is identical with living, not merely structurally parallel to it—is a question the present framework does not answer.

10.5 Deacon's Incomplete Nature

The cascade from thermodynamics through autocatalysis to sentience parallels the morphodynamics → teleodynamics → sentience hierarchy in Deacon. Both frameworks insist on continuity across levels and resist the introduction of new ontological primitives at any stage. Both treat the emergence of sentience as the thickening of operations already present in simpler systems rather than the appearance of a qualitatively new kind of process.

The divergence is again formal. Deacon's account remains qualitative and deliberately avoids mathematical formalization. The present framework provides formal machinery (the hypersphere dynamics, the consolidation operator, the reciprocation axes) that makes the "thickening" claim precise enough to generate predictions about where in the cascade each structural property emerges and what its measurable signatures should be.

11. Computational Verification

The framework's claims have been tested through a computational implementation of the core dynamics: Hadamard reception, additive update, renormalization on the unit hypersphere in ℂⁿ, with recurrence, an anticipatory operator, and consolidation sub-operations. Fourteen tests across five tiers verify the mechanical operations, regime predictions, learning dynamics, reciprocation structure, and anticipatory behavior. Twelve confirm the essay's claims. Two identify corrections or gaps that have been incorporated into the preceding sections.

This section reports the principal results. The implementation operates in ℂ³² unless otherwise noted.

11.1 Mechanical Correctness

T1.1 — Three reception regimes. The Hadamard product produces three qualitatively distinct dynamics from a single operation:

Regime Criterion Magnitude change Phase drift (rad)
Resonance Re(cᵢ) > 0 +0.0069 (grows) 0.056 (toward real axis)
Torque Re(cᵢ) < 0 or Im dominant −0.0068 (shrinks) 0.041 (rotates away)
Orthogonality cᵢ ≈ 0 −0.0024 (renorm drain) 0.000 (preserved)

The phase drift under resonance (0.056 rad) prompted a correction to the original formulation: resonance produces convergent rotation toward the arriving signal's phase, not pure scaling. The qualitative distinction between resonance (convergent rotation + growth) and torque (divergent rotation + shrinkage) remains valid but is now stated precisely (Section 2.3).

T1.2 — Emergent rigidity. A dominant dimension (|sᵢ| = 0.999) requires 55× more torque to produce the same angular displacement as a small dimension (|sᵢ| = 0.015). No separate rigidity mechanism is required; the geometry of renormalization on the unit hypersphere is sufficient.

T1.3 — Norm preservation. The unit norm is preserved to machine precision (maximum drift 3.3 × 10⁻¹⁶) over 10,000 steps with random inputs.

T1.4 — Concentration thins others. Sustained resonance at a single dimension for 200 steps drives all other components from 0.25 to effectively zero—a concentration ratio exceeding 10⁶.

11.2 Regime Predictions

T2.1 — Recurrent capture. Under pure resonance (v = conj(s) at every step), the participation ratio collapses from 18.6 to 1.0 within 1,000 steps. Perturbation rejection increases 600,000-fold: identical torque that produces 0.009 rad of angular displacement at step 50 produces effectively zero displacement at step 2,000. The system is frozen—locked along a single dimension, predicting exactly what it receives.

T2.2 — Dissipation. Under incoherent torque (varying random phase offsets), the participation ratio remains elevated (range 3.4–19.3), phases drift without stabilizing, and no dimension gains dominance.

A refinement: fixed-direction torque (constant +π/2 offset) does not produce dissipation—it creates systematic alignment along a rotated axis that still concentrates the tape. True dissipation requires that the opposition vary incoherently across time, lacking the regularity that would allow any dimension to accumulate magnitude. "Torque without resonance" should be read as "incoherent torque"—opposition from directions that vary faster than the system can track.

T2.3 — Operating regime. Under invariant signal (fixed-phase structure at 8 of 32 dimensions) plus noise (all dimensions) plus recurrence (delay = 5, weight = 0.6), the system achieves structured anisotropy: invariant dimensions average |sᵢ| = 0.208, noise dimensions average |sᵢ| = 0.013 (a 16× ratio), Gini coefficient 0.81, participation ratio stable at ~2.3, and the system remains responsive to perturbation (0.0055 rad displacement).

The operating regime does not emerge from balanced external input alone. It requires either non-resonant external variation (noise, surprise, novel structure in the world) or self-torque from recurrence—but recurrence alone is insufficient when the world provides pure resonance, because self-reception of a concentrated tape is self-resonance. The balancing force must come from the world's variation. The operating regime is a property of the system-world coupling.

11.3 Learning and Abstraction

T3.1 — Abstraction from variation. Invariant structure at 6 of 32 dimensions, uncorrelated noise at the remaining 26, with recurrence, for 3,000 steps. Invariant dimensions concentrate to 41× the magnitude of noise dimensions. Participation ratio drops from 14.5 to 3.0. The system extracts the invariant by selectively amplifying what recurs.

T3.2 — Novelty detection gap. Novel invariant structure introduced at previously unoccupied dimensions produces no detectable effect at existing dimensions through the Hadamard product. The measured difference in torque at existing dimensions (0.30 additional dimensions with torque, against baseline) is within noise.

This finding prompted a revision to the seeding mechanism (Section 3.1): novelty detection is rerouted through prediction error at the anticipatory operator, not through reception-level torque. The Hadamard product is dimensionally isolated; cross-dimensional information flow operates at the consolidation and anticipation timescales.

T3.3 — Co-activation correlation. Dimensions receiving identical input develop phase correlation of 1.0000; independently activated dimensions show correlation of 0.0136 (73× ratio). Co-activated dimensions are reliable candidates for the merging operation.

T3.4 — Fast vs. slow forgetting. Phase rotation under torque is reversible: a dimension rotated by 100 steps of torque recovers under renewed resonance. Basis pruning after prolonged starvation (|sᵢ| → 0 after 2,000 steps) is irreversible: once the dimension is removed, the system cannot recover the capacity through re-exposure. The distinction between losing an orientation and losing a capacity is confirmed.

11.4 Reciprocation and Recurrence

T4.1 — Thick vs. thin self-reception. Self-torque fraction increases with recurrence delay (0.743 at delay 1, 0.747 at delay 50). The effect is small under random-input conditions where the world provides high baseline torque (~74%). Under structured input with less world-torque, the delay effect would be more pronounced.

T4.2 — Reciprocation phase diagram. A scan across 27 configurations (3 delays × 3 recurrence weights × 3 breadth fractions) confirms that the operating regime occupies a bounded region:

  • Low recurrence weight (0.2), low-to-mid breadth (0.25–0.75): operating regime at all delays (PR 2–5, structured anisotropy, perturbation-responsive).
  • High recurrence weight (0.8+), high breadth (0.75+): dissipation (recurrence overwhelms world reception).
  • Mid-range: unstable oscillation between regimes.

11.5 Anticipation

T5.1 — Prediction error torque. After 1,000 steps of training, a regime change (phase shift at 8 dimensions) produces elevated prediction error (0.172 vs. post-adaptation 0.123, a 1.4× decay ratio). Prediction error partially concentrates at the shifted dimensions but distributes through renormalization coupling to all dimensions.

T5.2 — Habituation. Near-predictable input drives prediction error from 0.0701 (early) to 0.0695 (late). Perturbation spikes error to 0.1025 (1.48× baseline). Recovery returns error to 0.0000. The anticipatory operator learns regularity and detects deviation.

11.6 Open Computational Questions

Three questions raised by the implementation remain unresolved:

First, the consolidation operator has been verified at the substrate level (co-activation correlation, post-merger dynamics) but not as a fully autonomous cycle. A simulation in which the system detects merging conditions, executes the merge, and demonstrates qualitatively changed reception patterns would close the loop.

Second, the anticipatory operator's prediction of future input rather than future tape state is a departure from the original formulation that resolves a circularity (the tape is modified by prediction error, so predicting the tape chases its own corrections). Whether the two formulations can be reconciled—perhaps by separating prediction error correction from tape update at different timescales—is an open question.

Third, the operating regime has been verified at n = 32. Whether its character changes at much higher dimensionality—where renormalization coupling between dimensions weakens because gains at one dimension distribute across more others—is a scaling question that requires further investigation.

Appendix: A Worked Example in ℂ³

To illustrate the framework's operations concretely, consider a minimal system with n = 3: a tape s living on the unit hypersphere in ℂ³.

Initial state. Let the system begin with:

[ \mathbf{s}(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(1, 1, 1) ]

—uniform magnitude across all three dimensions, all phases aligned at zero. The system has no anisotropy. It is equally sensitive along all axes.

Reception. An incoming vector arrives, projected onto the basis:

[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} = (0.8, -0.3, 0.1i) ]

The Hadamard product is:

[ \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{received}} \odot \mathbf{s}(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(0.8, -0.3, 0.1i) ]

At dimension 1: Re(c₁) = 0.8/√3 > 0. Resonance. The system deepens along this axis, and the phase shifts slightly toward the real axis.

At dimension 2: Re(c₂) = −0.3/√3 < 0. Torque. The system is pushed away from its current orientation.

At dimension 3: c₃ = 0.1i/√3. Pure imaginary. Oblique torque—the system is pushed sideways, neither reinforced nor opposed. This is the regime that ℝⁿ cannot represent: lateral pressure that rotates without polarizing.

Update (η = 0.1). The unnormalized tape is:

[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{s}_1 &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} + 0.1 \cdot \frac{0.8}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{1.08}{\sqrt{3}} \ \tilde{s}_2 &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} + 0.1 \cdot \frac{-0.3}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{0.97}{\sqrt{3}} \ \tilde{s}_3 &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} + 0.1 \cdot \frac{0.1i}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{1 + 0.01i}{\sqrt{3}} \end{aligned} ]

After renormalization: s(1) has |s₁| > |s₂|, and s₃ has acquired a small imaginary component. The system has begun to differentiate—more sensitive along dimension 1, less along dimension 2, and rotated slightly in phase along dimension 3.

Emergent rigidity. After 50 receptions in which dimension 1 consistently resonates, |s₁| ≫ |s₂|, |s₃|. The norm is dominated by s₁. A torque of fixed magnitude at dimension 1 now produces much less angular displacement than the same torque at dimension 3. Dimension 1 has become hard through accumulated resonance—rejection is now the default outcome of torque at this axis. The system has developed a strong prior along dimension 1 without any explicit rigidity mechanism. The computational verification (T1.2) confirms the ratio: a dominant dimension is 55× harder to rotate than a small one.

Self-reception (thin). If the system receives its own prior tape after minimal change: c_self = s(49) ⊙ s(50) ≈ (|s₁|², |s₂|², |s₃|²). All components real and positive. Pure scaling. No self-torque. The system is in thin recurrence—deepening without learning.

Self-reception (thick). Now suppose that between t = 50 and t = 60, a series of strong torque events at dimension 2 rotates s₂ significantly in phase. When s(50) returns at t = 60: c₂ = s₂(50) · s₂(60) has a large imaginary component (the phases have diverged). Self-torque at dimension 2. The system's own past opposes its present at this axis. Meanwhile, c₁ ≈ |s₁|²—self-resonance at the hard axis, undisturbed. The system simultaneously reinforces its deep commitments and is challenged by its own recent history. This is thick recurrence: the engine is running.

Consolidation (merging). Suppose dimensions 1 and 3 have been consistently co-activated across 100 receptions—whenever dimension 1 resonates, dimension 3 also resonates, and when dimension 1 is torqued, dimension 3 is also torqued. The cross-correlation C₁₃ grows. The computational verification (T3.3) confirms this: co-activated dimensions develop phase correlation of 1.0000. When |C₁₃| exceeds θ_merge, a new dimension 4 is seeded, oriented along the conjunctive axis of dimensions 1 and 3. The system grows from ℂ³ to ℂ⁴. It can now detect the conjunction of structure at dimensions 1 and 3 as a single feature—an abstraction that neither dimension alone could represent.

Consolidation (pruning). Dimension 2, if it continues to decay after the torque events—receiving neither resonance nor further torque—eventually falls below θ_prune and is dropped. The system contracts from ℂ⁴ to ℂ³ again, but with a different basis: the original dimension 2 is gone, replaced by the conjunctive dimension. The system has lost the capacity to notice what dimension 2 registered, but gained the capacity to notice a higher-order invariant. This is the geometry of learning: every new distinction costs an old one, not by fiat, but by the redistribution of magnitude on the hypersphere through renormalization. The computational verification (T3.4) confirms the irreversibility: once a dimension is pruned, the capacity it represented cannot be recovered through re-exposure.
