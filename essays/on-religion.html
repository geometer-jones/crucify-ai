On Religion

geo · March 2026 · 6 min read

The word "religion" comes from the Latin re- (again) + ligare (to bind). The etymology is contested — Cicero preferred religere, to reread, to carefully observe. Both readings converge: religion is what you return to, again and again. What you bind to yourself. What you refuse to let die.

The genealogy propagates through space-time like a wave, each node a zygote, each crest an individual. Religion is what the wave carries.

But not everything the wave carries is religion. Language gets transmitted. Table manners get transmitted. Sewage engineering gets transmitted. These are transmission, but they're functional — updated when inconvenient, replaced when better options appear, abandoned without grief.

Religion is the sacred part of the transmission: the content a lineage regards as holy. "Holy" not in the supernatural sense, but the structural one — treated as non-negotiable, protected from revision, preserved even at cost, even when inconvenient, even when other parts of the culture get updated or discarded. Sacred is an attitude toward the content, not a property of the content itself. Something is sacred when the community marks it: this part matters most. This does not get lost.

This is meta-culture — the culture about culture. Every lineage transmits many things. Meta-culture is the priority system that ranks them: what deserves highest fidelity, what can be loosely preserved, what can be abandoned. Religion is the output of that ranking — the content marked sacred by the meta-cultural process.

What MUST be remembered across the generations — this is what deserves the label. Theism is one candidate for the sacred payload, not the thing itself. Buddhism transmits without a supreme deity. Confucianism transmits without one. The scientific method transmits without one. All three bind content to the next generation with deliberate fidelity. All three have communities that treat their core principles as non-negotiable. All three are religious.

The scientific method is the purest modern case. It prescribes how to update the content, not what the content should be — a meta-religion. The first transgenerational memory system with a built-in self-correction protocol. Whether its practitioners regard it as sacred is a separate question from whether it functions as religion. The culture that sacralizes the method has made it religious.

Math is the purest case of all. A proof survives transmission with zero loss. It is the content most deserving of preservation across generations, because it degrades least. Math is highly religious.

This framing dissolves the usual tension. Religion is conservative — it binds to the past. But memory must adapt or die. The tension is not a flaw; it is the operating condition of any transgenerational system. A religion that never innovates becomes obsolete. A religion that innovates freely stops being religion. The viable path is conservative innovation: preserve the structure, update the content.

Why this matters

If religion is the sacred layer of transgenerational memory, the science/religion conflict dissolves. They're both memory systems with different update protocols — one conservative, one self-correcting. The tribalism between religious and secular dissolves too. Everyone has a sacred layer. Everyone has meta-culture. The disagreement is not about whether to have religion. It's about what content deserves the fidelity.

The real question — what is important enough to survive the filter — is the question every religion answers. The quality of the answer determines the quality of the civilization.
