Abstraction

geo · April 2026 · 11 min read

Abstraction is not something minds do. It is something physics does.

A leaf takes sunlight and produces glucose. The input is high-dimensional — every photon has a wavelength, a phase, a polarization. The output is a single carbon ring carrying free energy. All the spectral detail is discarded. The leaf abstracts. Not by thinking. By compressing under constraint.

The same operation runs everywhere. A genome compresses billions of years of environmental variation into a sequence of base pairs. A neural network compresses millions of examples into a weight matrix. In each case: high-dimensional input, constrained system, lower-dimensional code that preserves what matters for what comes next.

This is abstraction. Not a cognitive act, not a feature of minds, but a physical process — one that runs in leaves, in genomes, in galaxies, long before anything like a mind existed to perform it.

The claim sounds simple. Its consequences are not. If abstraction is physical, then the question of why higher-level descriptions work — why thermodynamics doesn't need to track every molecule, why evolutionary theory doesn't need to track every base pair — has a physical answer, not merely a pragmatic one. The autonomy of higher-level descriptions is not a convenience we impose on the world. It is a structure the world deposits.

I. The Standard Picture and Its Gap

The dominant accounts of abstraction in philosophy of mind and cognitive science treat it as something agents do: they attend selectively, categorize, form concepts, extract features. Even accounts that try to naturalize abstraction — Millikan's teleosemantics, Dretske's information-theoretic semantics — keep the abstracting agent at the center. Abstraction is something a system does to information for its purposes.

This is not wrong as far as it goes. But it leaves a prior question unanswered: why does the world present compressible structure in the first place? Why do invariants exist to be abstracted? A complete account of abstraction must explain not just how agents extract regularities but why regularities are there to extract. That explanation, this paper argues, is physical.

The problem is familiar in philosophy of science as the problem of emergence and reduction. Batterman's work on asymptotic explanation shows that higher-level descriptions are sometimes not derivable from lower-level ones even in principle — the mathematical limits involved are singular, and the higher-level description captures something the lower-level description literally cannot express (Batterman 2001, 2002). Wimsatt's account of robustness argues that multiply-realizable structures are epistemically privileged precisely because they persist across changes in lower-level details (Wimsatt 1981). Ladyman and Ross's ontic structural realism holds that what is real at higher levels is pattern, not substance (Ladyman and Ross 2007).

Each of these accounts identifies the phenomenon: higher-level structure is real, autonomous, and not fully reducible to lower-level description. None gives a physical mechanism for why this is so. The compression account offered here does.

II. Compression

A gradient is a non-equilibrium condition: a difference in some intensive quantity across a system — energy, concentration, pressure, fitness. Gradients are what make things happen. At equilibrium, nothing happens.

The crucial identity: a gradient does not merely drive a system — it constrains it. A concentration gradient makes certain chemical states accessible and others not. A pressure gradient makes certain mechanical configurations stable and others not. The gradient reduces the effective state space. The driving force and the constraint are the same condition seen from two sides: from outside, the gradient dissipates toward equilibrium; from inside, the gradient limits what states the system can reach. Every active constraint is a gradient viewed as a limiter. Every gradient is a constraint viewed as a driver.

Compression is what a constrained system does. The constraint forces the system into a subset of possible configurations. Within that subset, some states are sustained and others suppressed. What survives is a lower-dimensional representation of the original state. The procedure is ordered: partition (constraint separates accessible from inaccessible), discriminate (within the accessible, some states are amplified, others suppressed), sustain (what survives is retained), deposit (the retained structure becomes part of the effective condition for what comes next).

No target needs to be specified in advance. The constraint does the selecting. What survives is what the constraint allowed to persist — the sufficient statistics of the constrained distribution.

At the quantum level, this operation has a precise physical implementation: the partial trace. When a composite system ρAB is traced over subsystem B, the result ρA = TrB(ρAB) is a lower-dimensional representation that preserves what is relevant to A while discarding the degrees of freedom carried by B. The partial trace is compression: lossy, system-relative, requiring no target. The output — the density matrix — is the only description of the system that ever arrives at the receiver. The full entanglement structure is never directly accessible. Only the abstraction arrives.

III. Interference: The Physical Mechanism

Compression runs on interference. At the quantum level, this is exact.

A system in superposition carries phases distributed across its degrees of freedom. When the system interacts with its environment — when a partial trace is performed — phases that align constructively produce larger amplitudes. Larger amplitudes are more likely to persist. This is structure: coherent content that survives the trace. Phases that misalign destructively cancel. Their amplitudes shrink toward zero. The information they carried is scattered into environmental degrees of freedom that no individual subsystem can recover. This is dissolution.

The same interaction produces both. Constructive interference preserves structure; destructive interference dissolves it. One partial trace, two outputs: the system's retained description and the environment's received noise.

This is also why the operation produces abstraction specifically, not just arbitrary compression. An invariant is a structural feature that recurs across instances. When the same pattern appears repeatedly, the corresponding phases align: constructive interference amplifies the invariant. An idiosyncratic feature — appearing once, in one configuration — finds its phases pointing in different directions when multiple instances are superposed, and cancels. The partial trace does not select for invariants because they are important. It selects for them because they resonate — they align and amplify. What doesn't recur doesn't resonate, and doesn't survive.

Abstraction is the extraction of what recurs from what doesn't, through resonance. Compression is the operation. Abstraction is what it produces.

This resonance account connects to Wimsatt's robustness analysis. Wimsatt argues that a feature is real to the extent that it is detectable across multiple independent procedures — robustness is the epistemic criterion for reality at higher levels. The compression account gives a physical basis for why robustness tracks reality: a feature that persists across multiple measurement contexts does so because it resonates — its phases align across instances, and constructive interference amplifies it regardless of which particular interaction performs the trace. Wimsatt's epistemic criterion and the physical mechanism pick out the same structures, for the same underlying reason.

At higher levels — thermodynamic, biological, social — the phase structure is present in the substrate but not directly visible in the effective description. A leaf does not present its photosynthetic compression as a partial trace on a Hilbert space. The information-theoretic description is valid at every scale: gradient → constraint → forced selection → compressed trace → retained invariant. Whether the quantum mechanism extends continuously to all scales is the open hypothesis. The compression pattern itself is the empirical anchor.

IV. Retained Structure

Call the output retained structure (or, more precisely, a compressed invariant): the abstraction that compression deposits. Not every persistent feature qualifies. What counts is structure produced under active constraint that remains consequential for successor dynamics. A random deposit that nothing downstream depends on is not a compressed invariant. The retained structure must be load-bearing: it must appear in the effective condition for what comes next.

The test is the factorization criterion: P(successor | full history, I) ≈ P(successor | I). If adding historical detail beyond the retained invariant I does not improve prediction of successor states, then I is a sufficient compression. If adding more detail does improve prediction, the compression was insufficient.

This criterion is a precisification of Dennett's real patterns test (Dennett 1991). Dennett argues that a pattern is real if it figures ineliminably in the best predictive account of a system's behavior — if you need to posit it to predict, it's real. The factorization criterion makes this operational: I is real at the successor-predictive level if and only if conditioning on I screens off the full history. The compression account grounds Dennett's pragmatic criterion in a physical mechanism: the patterns that survive the factorization test do so because they are the structures that resonated — the ones that the partial trace amplified and retained.

Retained structure is relational by construction. It describes what one system's compressed output means for another system's dynamics. There is no intrinsic retained structure independent of the successor relation, just as there is no intrinsic density matrix independent of the partial trace that produced it. This relationality is not a defect — it is what makes the account consistent with Ladyman and Ross's structural realism. What is real is not substance but pattern-in-relation; the compression account specifies the physical process by which those patterns are produced and maintained.

Three kinds of constraint, one process at different ages. Background constraints — conservation laws, fundamental constants — are the stage. Active constraints — gradients currently forcing selection — are the engine. Retained constraints — a folded protein, a gene-regulatory circuit, an institution — are compressed invariants that have become part of the effective condition for successor dynamics. Background, active, retained: not three kinds of things, but the same thing at different points in the construction's history.

V. The Duality

Wherever compression runs, it produces two things at once.

From the system's side: structure is deposited. Symmetries aligned, resonances sustained, invariants retained. The compressed trace carries the abstraction forward.

From the environment's side: degrees of freedom are dissipated. Symmetries cancelled, resonances destroyed, information scattered. The gradient has been partially consumed. This is entropy production.

These are not sequential. They are the same event. At the quantum level this is exact: the partial trace simultaneously compresses the system's description and dissipates the traced-out content. One operation, two irreducible descriptions.

At higher levels the pattern recurs. A leaf's photosynthesis simultaneously deposits chemical structure and dissipates the solar gradient. The same event, measured from inside and outside the leaf, reads as abstraction or as dissipation.

The duality is asymmetric. Entropy production occurs in every dissipating gradient. Retained structure arises only where compression runs — where the constraint produces selective differentiation. A bare rock under sunlight heats up: the solar gradient dissipates through diffusion alone. No structure is deposited. The rock diffuses. The leaf compresses. Both face the same gradient. The leaf produces more entropy, not less — the structured pathway dissipates the gradient faster. The second law does not tolerate structure reluctantly. Where compression occurs, structure arises because structure is the faster path to equilibrium.

This is a conditional claim: if structured systems dissipate gradients more efficiently than unstructured ones, then the second law selects for structure — not globally, not uniformly, but locally wherever gradients persist. Stars dissipate nuclear gradients faster than diffuse hydrogen. Ecosystems dissipate solar gradients faster than bare rock. Each level of structure opens access to gradients the previous level could not reach.

This asymmetry bears directly on Batterman's puzzle about emergence. Batterman shows that higher-level descriptions are sometimes indispensable — not merely convenient but genuinely capturing structure the lower-level description cannot express. The compression account provides a mechanism: higher-level descriptions capture the retained invariants, which are precisely the structures that survived the gradient-driven partial trace. The lower-level description cannot fully express these because the compression was lossy — the discarded degrees of freedom are gone. The higher-level description is autonomous not because we chose to describe things that way, but because the physical process that produced the structure was irreversible. Emergence, on this account, is what lossy compression looks like from above.

Retained structure degrades back into entropy. Every finite structure eventually loses its invariance as conditions change. Within the compression regime this produces an oscillation: compression deposits structure, structure degrades into entropy, entropy production constrains, forcing new compression. The oscillation is asymmetric — entropy production is continuous, structure deposition episodic — but each compression event deposits both. At equilibrium, neither occurs. Heat death is not entropy's triumph. It is the engine's exhaustion.

VI. When the Chain Breaks

The framework specifies when retained structure should not arise. Three failure modes:

Gradient without selective differentiation. All accessible trajectories dissipate equally. No pattern is preferentially stabilized. The gradient relaxes through diffusion alone. No compression, no retained structure.

Selective conditions without retainable invariants. Forcing is too irregular, too rapid, or too noisy for any feature to persist.

Candidate invariants without a stability window. Structure forms but constraints are too weak or perturbations too strong. The compression never stabilizes.

Missing any link breaks the chain. A leaf in the dark: stored chemical potential but no active solar gradient. A leaf under drought: stomata close, CO2 supply drops, photorespiration degrades rather than deposits. The framework predicts not only when retained structure arises but when it does not. This predictive asymmetry distinguishes it from accounts that merely redescribe persistence as emergence without specifying conditions of failure.

VII. Implications

The compression account has several consequences worth drawing out explicitly.

On the autonomy of higher-level descriptions. The account vindicates autonomy without mystery. Higher-level descriptions are autonomous because the physical process that produced the relevant structures was lossy and irreversible — the lower-level information was discarded, not merely ignored. Reduction fails not because of some further metaphysical ingredient but because the trace was performed. This gives Batterman's asymptotic results a physical interpretation: the singular limits he identifies correspond to cases where the compression was maximally lossy, leaving the higher-level description with no lower-level reconstruction available.

On multiple realizability. The compression account predicts that the same retained invariant can arise from different physical substrates, because what is retained is determined by the constraint structure, not the specific physical implementation. If two gradients impose equivalent constraints — forcing the same partition, the same discrimination, the same stabilization — they produce the same compressed invariant. Multiple realizability is not a brute fact about the world that special sciences must assume; it is a consequence of the gauge structure of compression. This connects to Fodor's argument for the irreducibility of special sciences (Fodor 1974) but grounds it physically rather than leaving it as a premise.

On the reality of higher-level patterns. The factorization criterion gives Dennett's real patterns a physical basis. A pattern is real at the successor-predictive level if and only if it is a compressed invariant — a structure that resonated across instances and survived the partial trace. The compression account does not merely agree with Dennett's pragmatic criterion; it explains why the criterion works. The patterns that pass the factorization test do so because they are the structures the physical world actually retained.

On what abstraction requires. The standard cognitive account requires a mind that attends, categorizes, and represents. The compression account requires only a gradient, a constrained system, and a partial trace. Minds are not prerequisites for abstraction — they are a particularly complex instance of it. The cognitive capacity for abstraction is not the origin of the phenomenon but a downstream product of the same physical process that runs in leaves and galaxies.

VIII. Status

The central claim: abstraction is a physical process. Compression under constraint — constructive interference preserving, destructive interference cancelling — produces lower-dimensional codes that carry task-relevant structure. This happens in photosynthesis, in evolution, in neural training. The operation is the partial trace at the quantum level; the pattern recurs at every scale where gradients force constrained systems to select what survives.

What is established: the gradient-constraint identity, the compression operation as a physical process, the identification of the partial trace as its quantum-level implementation, the factorization criterion as a test for sufficient compression, and the duality of compression and dissipation as two descriptions of one event.

What is hypothesized: that the quantum-level identity extends to higher scales — that the same mechanism responsible for decoherence and the emergence of classicality is responsible for the robustness of biological and social structures. This is a substantive empirical claim, not a definition. It could be false. The compression pattern recurs across scales; whether it recurs because the underlying mechanism is the same, or because different mechanisms produce formally similar results, is an open question.

What this paper does not do: derive the spectral formalism, establish the Friston-Prigogine correspondence, or provide a quantitative account of how compression efficiency scales with level of organization. Those are extensions of the framework, not prerequisites for the philosophical argument. The argument stands or falls on whether compression under constraint — gradient-driven, partial-trace-implemented, interference-selected — provides a better account of why higher-level descriptions work than accounts that begin with minds or with bare emergence. The case made here is that it does.
